# Regional Market Analysis: Northeast Commercial Real Estate

The commercial real estate market in the northeastern United States showed mixed performance during the third quarter of 2025, reflecting broader economic uncertainty and evolving workplace trends. This report summarizes key developments across three major property segments: office, industrial, and retail.

Office vacancy rates continued their gradual decline, falling to 14.2 percent from 15.8 percent in the previous quarter. However, this improvement was concentrated in Class A properties in central business districts, where technology and financial services firms have been expanding their footprints. Class B and Class C office buildings, particularly those in suburban locations, continued to struggle with vacancy rates exceeding 20 percent. Landlords of these properties are increasingly investing in renovations and amenity upgrades to attract tenants.

The industrial segment remained the strongest performer. Warehouse and distribution center vacancy rates held steady at 3.1 percent, the lowest level in over a decade. Demand was driven by continued growth in e-commerce fulfillment operations and nearshoring trends that have increased domestic warehousing requirements. Average asking rents for industrial space rose 8 percent year-over-year to $12.40 per square foot.

Retail properties showed signs of stabilization after several challenging years. Vacancy rates improved slightly to 6.8 percent, supported by the expansion of experiential retail concepts such as fitness centers, entertainment venues, and food halls that have replaced traditional anchor tenants in many shopping centers. Grocery-anchored strip centers continued to outperform enclosed malls, with vacancy rates of 4.1 percent compared with 11.3 percent for regional malls.

Looking ahead, rising interest rates may slow new construction activity, potentially providing support for existing property values by limiting new supply.

## Q1. Which property segment had the lowest vacancy rate?
- A. Class A office buildings
- B. Industrial warehouses and distribution centers
- C. Grocery-anchored strip centers
- D. Regional enclosed malls
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Industrial vacancy rates were 3.1 percent, the lowest of any segment discussed. Grocery-anchored strip centers were 4.1 percent, and other segments were higher.

## Q2. What can be inferred about the future of Class B suburban office buildings?
- A. They face continued challenges unless significant improvements are made.
- B. They will likely be demolished within the next year.
- C. They have already been fully converted to residential properties.
- D. Their vacancy rates are declining faster than Class A buildings.
- Answer: A
- Explanation: With vacancy rates exceeding 20 percent, landlords "are increasingly investing in renovations and amenity upgrades to attract tenants," suggesting these properties face ongoing difficulties.

## Q3. What has contributed to the retail sector's improved performance?
- A. Experiential retail concepts replacing traditional anchor tenants
- B. Government subsidies for shopping mall construction
- C. A ban on online shopping in the northeastern states
- D. Significant reductions in commercial property taxes
- Answer: A
- Explanation: The report attributes retail stabilization to "the expansion of experiential retail concepts such as fitness centers, entertainment venues, and food halls."

## Q4. The word "nearshoring" in the industrial segment discussion most likely refers to:
- A. Relocating business operations to nearby countries or domestic locations
- B. Building warehouses near ocean shorelines for shipping purposes
- C. Selling industrial properties to neighboring businesses
- D. Reducing the distance between retail stores and customers
- Answer: A
- Explanation: "Nearshoring" refers to moving production or operations closer to home markets, which in context has "increased domestic warehousing requirements."
