# How Corporate Innovation Labs Are Transforming Traditional Industries

In an era when disruption can render established business models obsolete within a few years, a growing number of companies in traditional industries are establishing dedicated innovation laboratories. These units, often physically and organizationally separated from core business operations, are tasked with exploring emerging technologies and developing new revenue streams before competitive pressures make change unavoidable.

Kendrick Consulting's 2025 Global Innovation Survey found that 47 percent of companies in sectors such as manufacturing, financial services, and logistics now operate some form of innovation lab, up from just 19 percent in 2020. The most successful labs share several characteristics: they maintain a distinct budget not subject to quarterly performance pressures, they recruit talent from outside the industry to bring fresh perspectives, and they collaborate directly with startup ecosystems and academic institutions.

However, critics point to a significant failure rate. Approximately 60 percent of corporate innovation labs launched between 2018 and 2022 were either closed or significantly downsized within four years. Professor Alan Richter of the Warwick Business School argues that many labs fail because they lack a clear mechanism for integrating their outputs into the parent company's operations. "Innovation in isolation is merely experimentation," he notes. "Without a structured pathway from prototype to production, these labs become expensive showcases with no tangible business impact."

Companies that have overcome this challenge typically employ dedicated integration teams that bridge the gap between the lab and the main organization. Ferris Industrial Group, for example, credits its integration unit with successfully commercializing three lab-developed technologies that now generate over $400 million in combined annual revenue. The key, according to Ferris CEO Jeanette Moreau, is ensuring that innovation is treated not as a side project but as a core strategic function with executive accountability.

## Q1. What is the main trend described in the article?
- A. Traditional industries are increasingly investing in dedicated innovation units.
- B. Innovation labs are being replaced by outsourced research contracts.
- C. Financial services companies are abandoning technology development.
- D. Startup ecosystems are declining in relevance to large corporations.
- Answer: A
- Explanation: The article describes "a growing number of companies in traditional industries" establishing innovation labs, with adoption rising from 19% to 47% between 2020 and 2025.

## Q2. According to Professor Richter, why do many innovation labs fail?
- A. They hire too many employees from outside the industry.
- B. They lack processes for turning their work into actual business products.
- C. They receive excessive funding that leads to wasteful spending.
- D. They focus too heavily on short-term financial performance.
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Professor Richter states that labs fail because "they lack a clear mechanism for integrating their outputs into the parent company's operations."

## Q3. What distinguishes the most successful innovation labs?
- A. They are managed entirely by the company's existing senior leadership.
- B. They operate under the same budget constraints as other departments.
- C. They have independent budgets, external talent, and partnerships with startups and academia.
- D. They focus exclusively on improving current products rather than developing new ones.
- Answer: C
- Explanation: Successful labs "maintain a distinct budget," "recruit talent from outside the industry," and "collaborate directly with startup ecosystems and academic institutions."

## Q4. What can be inferred from the Ferris Industrial Group example?
- A. Integration teams are unnecessary if the lab has sufficient funding.
- B. Bridging the gap between innovation and core operations is critical for commercial success.
- C. Only manufacturing companies can benefit from innovation labs.
- D. Lab-developed technologies rarely generate meaningful revenue.
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Ferris credits its integration unit with commercializing three technologies generating $400 million, demonstrating that bridging innovation and operations is essential for turning lab work into business value.
