# Supply Chain Regionalization: A Structural Shift in Global Trade

The past several years have witnessed a fundamental reorientation of global supply chain strategy. Where multinational corporations once prioritized cost minimization through extended networks spanning dozens of countries, many are now pursuing a regionalized approach that emphasizes resilience, speed, and geopolitical risk mitigation.

According to the World Trade Policy Institute's 2025 annual survey, 54 percent of large manufacturers reported having relocated at least one production facility closer to their primary end markets since 2022. This trend, frequently described as "nearshoring" or "friend-shoring," has been accelerated by the disruptions of the pandemic era, rising shipping costs, and increasing trade tensions between major economic blocs.

The economic implications are substantial. Countries in Central and Eastern Europe have emerged as significant beneficiaries, attracting manufacturing investment from firms seeking alternatives to distant Asian production hubs. Mexico has similarly experienced a surge in foreign direct investment, with manufacturing FDI reaching a record $22 billion in 2025, driven largely by North American companies restructuring their supply chains.

However, economists caution that regionalization carries trade-offs. Professor Miriam Hsu of the London School of Economics notes that while shorter supply chains reduce vulnerability to global disruptions, they also increase production costs by an estimated 8 to 15 percent. "Companies are essentially paying an insurance premium for reliability," she explains. "The question is whether consumer markets will absorb these higher costs or whether they will erode corporate margins."

Furthermore, the shift may exacerbate economic inequality between regions. Nations that previously served as low-cost manufacturing centers, particularly in Southeast Asia and sub-Saharan Africa, face the prospect of declining investment flows as production moves closer to wealthy consumer markets. Policymakers in affected regions are increasingly advocating for trade agreements and infrastructure investments designed to maintain their competitiveness.

## Q1. What is the primary driver behind supply chain regionalization?
- A. A desire to reduce environmental impact
- B. The need for greater resilience against disruptions and geopolitical risks
- C. Government mandates requiring domestic production
- D. A decline in consumer demand for imported products
- Answer: B
- Explanation: The article identifies the shift as emphasizing "resilience, speed, and geopolitical risk mitigation," driven by pandemic disruptions, shipping costs, and trade tensions.

## Q2. Which regions have benefited from the nearshoring trend?
- A. Sub-Saharan Africa and Southeast Asia
- B. Central and Eastern Europe and Mexico
- C. Western Europe and Japan
- D. The Middle East and Central Asia
- Answer: B
- Explanation: The article states that "Central and Eastern Europe have emerged as significant beneficiaries" and Mexico saw record manufacturing FDI of $22 billion in 2025.

## Q3. What does Professor Hsu mean by "paying an insurance premium for reliability"?
- A. Companies are purchasing insurance policies to cover supply chain losses.
- B. Firms accept higher production costs in exchange for more dependable supply chains.
- C. Manufacturers are investing in new insurance products for their workers.
- D. Regionalized supply chains eliminate all production risks entirely.
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Professor Hsu notes that shorter supply chains "increase production costs by an estimated 8 to 15 percent" but reduce vulnerability, meaning companies pay more for greater reliability.

## Q4. What can be inferred about the long-term impact on developing nations?
