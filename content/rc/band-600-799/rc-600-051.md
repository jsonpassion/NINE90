# Climate Risk Reshapes Commercial Insurance Market

The commercial insurance industry is undergoing a significant transformation as climate-related risks increasingly influence underwriting decisions, pricing models, and coverage availability. According to the Global Insurance Risk Council, insured losses from climate-related events exceeded $130 billion in 2025, the third consecutive year above $100 billion, fundamentally altering how insurers assess and price risk.

The impact is most visible in coastal and wildfire-prone regions. Commercial property insurance premiums in southeastern coastal states have increased by an average of 32 percent over the past two years, while several major insurers have reduced their exposure by declining to renew policies for properties in the highest-risk zones. In California, three of the state's ten largest commercial property insurers have announced plans to limit new policy issuance in wildfire-adjacent areas.

These market dynamics are forcing businesses to reconsider their risk management strategies. Harborview Development Corporation, which owns a portfolio of 24 commercial properties along the Atlantic seaboard, reported that its annual insurance costs increased from $8.2 million to $11.6 million between 2023 and 2025. In response, the company invested $14 million in property resilience upgrades, including reinforced roofing systems, flood barriers, and backup power installations.

"The investment in physical resilience has been essential," said Harborview's risk management director, Lisa Montoya. "After completing the upgrades, we were able to negotiate premium reductions of approximately 12 percent and secure coverage terms that several carriers had initially refused to offer."

Insurance industry analysts expect the trend of rising premiums and selective coverage to continue. They advise businesses in high-risk areas to proactively invest in resilience measures, explore alternative risk transfer mechanisms such as catastrophe bonds, and engage insurers early in the renewal process to allow sufficient time for negotiation.

## Q1. What trend is described in the insurance market?
- A. Climate-related losses are declining, leading to lower premiums.
- B. Government regulations have stabilized insurance pricing across all regions.
- C. Insurance companies are expanding into new markets due to increased profits.
- D. Rising climate losses are driving higher premiums and reduced coverage availability.
- Answer: D
- Explanation: The article describes insured losses exceeding $130 billion, premium increases of 32 percent, and insurers declining to renew policies in high-risk zones.

## Q2. How did Harborview respond to rising insurance costs?
- A. It sold all its coastal properties and relocated inland.
- B. It invested in physical property resilience upgrades that led to premium reductions.
- C. It decided to operate without insurance coverage entirely.
- D. It filed lawsuits against its insurance providers.
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Harborview "invested $14 million in property resilience upgrades" and subsequently "negotiated premium reductions of approximately 12 percent."

## Q3. What can be inferred about the future of commercial property insurance?
- A. Premiums will decrease significantly as insurers compete for clients.
- B. Businesses in high-risk areas will face continued challenges obtaining affordable coverage.
- C. Climate-related losses will have no further impact on insurance markets.
- D. All insurers will be required by law to offer coverage in every location.
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Analysts "expect the trend of rising premiums and selective coverage to continue" and advise businesses to proactively invest in resilience.

## Q4. The term "catastrophe bonds" most likely refers to:
- A. Government savings bonds that mature during natural disasters
- B. Financial instruments that transfer catastrophic risk from insurers to capital market investors
- C. Penalty fees charged to businesses that fail to maintain insurance coverage
- D. Construction materials designed to withstand extreme weather events
- Answer: B
- Explanation: In context, catastrophe bonds are described as "alternative risk transfer mechanisms," meaning financial tools that shift catastrophic loss risk to investors.
