# Pharmaceutical Industry Report

Biosimilar Market Dynamics and Their Impact on Pharmaceutical Pricing Strategies

The biosimilar market has emerged as one of the most consequential forces reshaping pharmaceutical economics, with global biosimilar sales reaching $42 billion in 2025, a 31 percent increase from the previous year. As patents for several blockbuster biological drugs continue to expire, the competitive landscape is shifting in ways that require both originator companies and biosimilar manufacturers to recalibrate their commercial strategies.

The economic impact of biosimilar competition varies significantly by therapeutic category and geography. In the oncology segment, biosimilar versions of trastuzumab and rituximab have achieved market penetration rates of 65 percent and 58 percent respectively in European markets within three years of launch, driving price reductions of 40 to 55 percent. In contrast, biosimilar penetration in the immunology category, despite the availability of multiple adalimumab biosimilars, has reached only 35 percent in the same markets, partly due to effective brand loyalty programs and physician switching reluctance.

Originator companies are employing increasingly sophisticated defensive strategies. Value-based contracting arrangements, in which pricing is linked to patient outcomes, have proven effective in differentiating branded products from biosimilar alternatives. Several major biologics companies have also pursued authorized biosimilar partnerships, in which they license a biosimilar version of their own product to a partner company, enabling them to capture a portion of the biosimilar market rather than ceding it entirely to competitors. This strategy has been particularly successful in the insulin market, where authorized biosimilars now account for 22 percent of total biosimilar insulin sales.

For healthcare systems, the continued expansion of the biosimilar market represents a critical opportunity to manage pharmaceutical spending. The European Commission estimates that biosimilar adoption has saved European healthcare systems approximately $15 billion between 2020 and 2025, with projected cumulative savings of $50 billion by 2030.
