# Small Businesses Face Growing Cybersecurity Threats

While cybersecurity has long been a priority for large corporations, small and medium-sized businesses are increasingly finding themselves targets of sophisticated cyberattacks. The National Cybersecurity Alliance reports that 43 percent of all cyberattacks in 2025 targeted businesses with fewer than 500 employees, up from 28 percent five years ago.

The financial consequences can be devastating. The average cost of a data breach for a small business reached $164,000 in 2025, a figure that can be existentially threatening for companies with limited financial reserves. Beyond direct financial losses, breaches often result in reputational damage, loss of customer trust, and regulatory penalties that compound the initial impact.

Riverside Accounting Services, a 35-employee firm based in Denver, experienced a ransomware attack in March 2025 that encrypted its client database and financial records. The attackers demanded a ransom of $85,000 in cryptocurrency. The firm's managing partner, Susan Cho, decided not to pay the ransom and instead engaged a cybersecurity recovery firm to restore operations from backup systems.

"The recovery process took 11 days, during which we could not serve our clients," Cho recalled. "The total cost, including the recovery firm's fees, lost revenue, and required security upgrades, exceeded $210,000. We learned that our backup systems, while they existed, had not been tested in over a year and were only partially current."

Industry experts recommend that small businesses adopt several foundational cybersecurity practices: implementing multi-factor authentication for all business systems, conducting regular employee security awareness training, maintaining and regularly testing data backup systems, and purchasing cybersecurity insurance. A basic cybersecurity program incorporating these elements can typically be implemented for between $5,000 and $15,000 annually, a fraction of the potential cost of a successful attack.

## Q1. What trend does the article describe regarding cyberattacks?
- A. A growing proportion of cyberattacks are targeting small and medium-sized businesses.
- B. Large corporations are increasingly targeted while small businesses are safe.
- C. The total number of cyberattacks has decreased significantly since 2020.
- D. Government agencies are the primary targets of cybercriminals.
- Answer: A
- Explanation: Attacks targeting businesses with fewer than 500 employees rose from 28 to 43 percent of all cyberattacks.

## Q2. What critical failure did Riverside Accounting discover during recovery?
- A. Their cybersecurity insurance policy had expired without renewal.
- B. The ransomware had also infected their backup systems completely.
- C. Their employees had deliberately disabled the security software.
- D. Their backup systems had not been tested recently and were only partially current.
- Answer: D
- Explanation: Cho stated "our backup systems had not been tested in over a year and were only partially current."

## Q3. What can be inferred about the relationship between cybersecurity investment and breach costs?
- A. Investing in cybersecurity provides no meaningful protection against attacks.
- B. A relatively modest annual investment can prevent far larger potential losses.
- C. Only businesses that spend over $100,000 annually can prevent breaches.
- D. Cybersecurity insurance eliminates all financial risk from attacks.
- Answer: B
- Explanation: A basic program costs $5,000-$15,000 annually, while the average breach costs $164,000 — suggesting prevention is far cheaper than recovery.
