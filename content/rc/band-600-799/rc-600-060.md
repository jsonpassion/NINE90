# New Trade Agreement Opens Markets for Agricultural Exporters

The recently ratified Pacific Agricultural Trade Accord is expected to create significant opportunities for agricultural exporters in the United States, Australia, and New Zealand by reducing tariffs and harmonizing phytosanitary standards across 11 participating countries. The agreement, which took effect on October 1, 2025, is projected to increase agricultural trade among member nations by $18 billion annually within five years.

Under the terms of the accord, tariffs on major commodity categories will be reduced in three phases over six years. Phase one, effective immediately, eliminates tariffs on grains and oilseeds, which previously ranged from 8 to 15 percent depending on the importing country. Phase two, beginning in 2027, will reduce tariffs on dairy and meat products by 50 percent. Phase three, scheduled for 2031, will eliminate remaining tariffs on all agricultural products.

Perhaps more significant than the tariff reductions is the harmonization of food safety and plant health standards. Previously, differing regulatory requirements among member countries created substantial compliance costs for exporters. Under the accord, a single set of phytosanitary standards will apply, potentially saving exporters an estimated $2.4 billion annually in testing, certification, and product reformulation costs.

American soybean producers stand to benefit considerably. The United States is the world's second-largest soybean exporter, and the accord's immediate elimination of soybean tariffs in several Southeast Asian markets is expected to increase U.S. soybean exports by 12 to 18 percent within two years. The American Soybean Association described the agreement as "the most significant trade development for our industry in the past decade."

However, some domestic producers in importing countries have expressed concern that lower tariffs will expose them to competition from larger, more efficient foreign producers, potentially threatening rural livelihoods. The accord includes provisions for temporary safeguard measures if import surges cause or threaten serious injury to domestic industries.

## Q1. What are the two main benefits of the trade accord?
- A. New shipping routes and reduced transportation costs
- B. Increased domestic production subsidies and reduced export quotas
- C. Elimination of all agricultural regulations and tariff increases
- D. Reduced tariffs and harmonized food safety standards
- Answer: D
- Explanation: The accord reduces tariffs in three phases and harmonizes "food safety and plant health standards" to create a single set of requirements.

## Q2. When will tariffs on dairy and meat products be reduced?
- A. Beginning in 2027
- B. Immediately upon the accord taking effect
- C. In 2031
- D. Dairy and meat products are excluded from the accord
- Answer: A
- Explanation: "Phase two, beginning in 2027, will reduce tariffs on dairy and meat products by 50 percent."

## Q3. What can be inferred about the accord's impact on smaller domestic producers in importing countries?
- A. They will benefit from increased export opportunities to the United States.
- B. They are not affected because the accord only covers commodity crops.
- C. They are fully protected by permanent trade barriers under the agreement.
- D. They could face significant competitive pressure from larger foreign producers.
- Answer: D
- Explanation: Producers "expressed concern that lower tariffs will expose them to competition from larger, more efficient foreign producers, potentially threatening rural livelihoods."

## Q4. The word "phytosanitary" most likely relates to:
