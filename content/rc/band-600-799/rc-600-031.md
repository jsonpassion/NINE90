# Supply Chain Diversification Gains Momentum Among Manufacturers

The global manufacturing sector is accelerating efforts to diversify supply chains following a series of disruptions that exposed vulnerabilities in concentrated sourcing strategies. According to a survey released by the Industrial Procurement Institute, 67 percent of manufacturers with annual revenues exceeding $500 million have initiated supply chain restructuring programs since 2023.

The survey, which included responses from 480 companies across 12 countries, found that the average manufacturer now sources critical components from suppliers in 4.3 different countries, up from 2.8 in 2020. The shift has been particularly pronounced in the electronics and automotive sectors, where companies experienced the most severe disruptions during the pandemic period.

Hartfield Manufacturing, a producer of industrial control systems based in Ohio, exemplifies this trend. The company previously sourced 85 percent of its semiconductor components from a single supplier in East Asia. After experiencing a six-month supply interruption in 2022 that cost the company an estimated $34 million in lost revenue, Hartfield implemented a dual-sourcing strategy that now distributes procurement across three suppliers in different regions.

"The transition was not without cost," explained Hartfield's procurement director, Alan Chambers. "Our component costs increased by approximately 9 percent due to the loss of volume discounts with our original supplier. However, we conducted a risk-adjusted analysis that showed the long-term savings from reduced disruption exposure more than justified the higher unit costs."

Industry analysts note that supply chain diversification also carries operational complexities. Managing relationships with multiple suppliers across different regulatory environments requires additional staffing, more sophisticated logistics coordination, and greater investment in quality assurance systems. Smaller manufacturers, which may lack the resources for such investments, face particular challenges in implementing diversification strategies.

## Q1. What prompted the acceleration of supply chain diversification?
- A. Government mandates requiring manufacturers to use domestic suppliers
- B. Disruptions that revealed weaknesses in concentrated sourcing strategies
- C. A decline in the quality of components from international suppliers
- D. The bankruptcy of several major logistics companies
- Answer: B
- Explanation: The article states manufacturers are diversifying "following a series of disruptions that exposed vulnerabilities in concentrated sourcing strategies."

## Q2. How much did Hartfield's component costs increase after diversification?
- A. 6 percent
- B. 85 percent
- C. 34 percent
- D. 9 percent
- Answer: D
- Explanation: Chambers stated that "component costs increased by approximately 9 percent due to the loss of volume discounts."

## Q3. What can be inferred about smaller manufacturers' ability to diversify?
- A. They can diversify more easily because they have simpler supply chains.
- B. They are exempt from supply chain regulations that apply to larger companies.
- C. They face greater obstacles due to limited resources for managing complex supplier networks.
- D. They have already completed their diversification programs.
- Answer: C
- Explanation: The article notes that diversification requires "additional staffing, more sophisticated logistics coordination, and greater investment" and that "smaller manufacturers, which may lack the resources for such investments, face particular challenges."

## Q4. The phrase "risk-adjusted analysis" most likely refers to:
- A. A customer satisfaction survey measuring product quality
- B. A financial audit conducted by an external accounting firm
- C. A government-mandated safety inspection of manufacturing facilities
- D. An evaluation that factors in the probability and cost of potential supply disruptions
- Answer: D
- Explanation: In context, Hartfield weighed the higher unit costs against "reduced disruption exposure," meaning the analysis accounted for the financial risks of supply chain interruptions.
