# Central Bank Digital Currencies: Implications for Commercial Banking

The accelerating development of central bank digital currencies ("CBDCs") by major economies is prompting fundamental reassessment of the commercial banking business model. As of 2025, 11 countries have launched CBDCs, 21 are conducting pilot programs, and 68 are in advanced research phases. The prospect of a digital euro, digital pound, and potential digital dollar has particularly concentrated attention on the implications for the banking sectors of the world's largest economies.

The central concern for commercial banks is disintermediation. In the current monetary architecture, the public accesses central bank money exclusively through physical cash, while digital money is held as commercial bank deposits — liabilities of private institutions — rather than as direct claims on the central bank. A CBDC would for the first time provide the public with access to digital central bank money, potentially reducing reliance on commercial bank deposits as a store of value and medium of exchange.

The magnitude of potential deposit displacement is a subject of intense debate. The Bank of England's analysis suggests that a digital pound could attract £100 to £250 billion in deposits from the commercial banking system, representing 5 to 12 percent of the sector's total deposit base. This migration would reduce banks' lowest-cost funding source and could increase their weighted average cost of funding by 20 to 40 basis points, with significant implications for lending margins and profitability.

Central banks are aware of these risks and have generally proposed design features intended to limit deposit displacement. The European Central Bank's digital euro design includes a holding limit of €3,000 per individual, above which funds would automatically be swept back to a linked commercial bank account. The Bank of England has proposed a similar holding limit of £10,000 to £20,000, while emphasizing that the digital pound is intended to complement rather than replace commercial bank deposits.

Critics argue that holding limits create an inherently awkward monetary instrument — too constrained for meaningful use as a savings vehicle, yet potentially sufficient to disrupt the deposit funding model upon which commercial banking is predicated. Proponents counter that the primary purpose of a retail CBDC is to serve as a reliable digital payment instrument rather than a savings vehicle, and that holding limits appropriately balance innovation with financial stability.

## Q1. What is the primary concern for commercial banks regarding CBDCs?
- A. CBDCs would make it impossible for banks to process international payments.
- B. CBDCs would require banks to replace all their existing technology systems.
- C. Disintermediation — the potential migration of deposits away from commercial banks to CBDC holdings.
- D. Central banks would begin offering commercial loans in competition with private banks.
- Answer: C
- Explanation: "The central concern for commercial banks is disintermediation," with CBDCs potentially "reducing reliance on commercial bank deposits as a store of value."

## Q2. How does the ECB propose to limit deposit displacement?
- A. By prohibiting any individual from holding digital euros
- B. By requiring commercial banks to match CBDC interest rates
- C. By setting a €3,000 per person holding limit with automatic sweep-back to bank accounts
- D. By limiting CBDC usage to government payments only
- Answer: C
- Explanation: "The digital euro design includes a holding limit of €3,000 per individual, above which funds would automatically be swept back to a linked commercial bank account."

## Q3. What can be inferred about the tension between CBDC design goals?
- A. All stakeholders agree that CBDCs should have unlimited holding capacity.
- B. There is an inherent tradeoff between making CBDCs useful enough to serve their purpose and limiting their potential to disrupt the banking system.
- C. CBDCs are designed exclusively for large institutional transactions.
- D. Financial stability concerns have been completely resolved through holding limits.
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Critics call holding limits "inherently awkward" while proponents say they "appropriately balance innovation with financial stability" — revealing the fundamental tension.

## Q4. The phrase "weighted average cost of funding" refers to:
- A. The blended interest rate a bank pays across all its funding sources, weighted by their proportions
- B. The total interest expense of the central bank
- C. The cost of printing physical banknotes for the central bank
- D. The average salary paid to bank employees across all departments
- Answer: A
- Explanation: If deposits (cheap funding) are displaced, banks must rely more on costlier funding sources, increasing their "weighted average cost of funding by 20 to 40 basis points."
