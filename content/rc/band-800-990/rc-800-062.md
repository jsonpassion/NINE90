# Sovereign Debt Restructuring Analysis

Sovereign Debt Restructuring in Emerging Markets: Lessons from Recent Experience and Implications for Institutional Investors

The period from 2023 to 2025 witnessed a significant increase in sovereign debt restructuring activity among emerging market economies, with six countries completing or initiating formal restructuring processes. This development carries profound implications for institutional investors holding emerging market debt instruments, particularly as the restructuring frameworks have evolved in ways that alter the traditional calculus of risk and recovery.

The fundamental challenge in contemporary sovereign debt restructuring lies in the increasing complexity of creditor composition. Unlike the bank-dominated lending structures of previous decades, current sovereign debt is dispersed across a heterogeneous group of creditors including multilateral institutions, bilateral official creditors (both Paris Club members and non-traditional lenders such as Chinese state-owned banks), international bondholders, and domestic financial institutions. This fragmentation has complicated negotiation processes, as evidenced by the average restructuring timeline extending from 14 months in the 2010-2019 period to 23 months in recent cases, with the prolonged uncertainty imposing additional economic costs on debtor nations.

The Common Framework for Debt Treatments, endorsed by the G20, was designed to address these coordination challenges by establishing principles for comparable treatment across creditor classes. However, its implementation has been uneven. Of the three restructurings completed under the Framework, only one achieved the targeted debt relief within the originally projected timeline. The remaining cases were delayed by disputes between official bilateral creditors over the definition of "comparable treatment" — specifically, whether debt reduction for commercial creditors should be calibrated against the concessionality embedded in official sector loans or against their nominal face value.

For institutional investors, the evolving restructuring landscape necessitates a fundamental reassessment of emerging market sovereign credit analysis. Traditional metrics such as debt-to-GDP ratios and external financing requirements remain necessary but insufficient. Portfolio managers must now also evaluate the composition and concentration of a sovereign's creditor base, the applicability and enforceability of collective action clauses in bond documentation, the debtor country's geopolitical relationships that influence the willingness of key bilateral creditors to participate constructively, and the capacity of domestic institutions to implement the economic adjustment programs that typically accompany restructuring agreements.

Recovery rates in recent restructurings have averaged 52 cents on the dollar for external commercial creditors, though the dispersion around this average is wide, ranging from 35 to 71 cents depending on the specific circumstances. Notably, creditors who maintained positions through the restructuring process and participated in new-money facilities have, in several instances, achieved total returns that exceeded those of creditors who sold distressed positions at the onset of restructuring negotiations.

## Q1. What has changed about the creditor composition in sovereign debt?
- A. All lending is now exclusively through multilateral institutions.
- B. Creditors have become more homogeneous and easier to coordinate.
- C. Debt is dispersed across a heterogeneous group including multilateral, bilateral, bondholder, and domestic creditors.
- D. Private bondholders have been entirely replaced by government lenders.
- Answer: C
- Explanation: The passage notes 'current sovereign debt is dispersed across a heterogeneous group of creditors' including multiple categories.

## Q2. What has been the primary obstacle to the Common Framework's effectiveness?
- A. Insufficient participation by debtor countries
- B. Disputes between bilateral creditors over the definition of comparable treatment
- C. Opposition from multilateral institutions to debt relief
- D. Lack of endorsement by major economic powers
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Cases were 'delayed by disputes between official bilateral creditors over the definition of comparable treatment.'

## Q3. What additional factors must portfolio managers now evaluate beyond traditional metrics?

- Answer: C
- Explanation: Managers must evaluate 'the composition and concentration of a sovereign's creditor base, the applicability of collective action clauses, geopolitical relationships, and domestic institutional capacity.'
