# Indemnification and Liability Allocation in Complex Commercial Transactions

This analysis examines the indemnification provisions contained in the Asset Purchase Agreement ("APA") dated October 1, 2025, pursuant to which Consolidated Industrial Holdings ("Buyer") acquired the chemical processing division of Westfield Manufacturing Group ("Seller") for a purchase price of $1.4 billion.

Article 8 of the APA establishes a tiered indemnification framework governing post-closing liability allocation. Section 8.1 provides that the Seller shall indemnify the Buyer against losses arising from: (a) any breach of the Seller's representations and warranties concerning the condition, operations, and compliance status of the acquired business; (b) any pre-closing environmental liabilities, including remediation obligations arising from contamination that occurred or originated prior to the closing date; and (c) any pre-closing tax liabilities of the acquired business.

The indemnification obligations are subject to several limitations. A deductible (or "basket") of $14 million applies to claims under Section 8.1(a), meaning the Buyer bears the first $14 million in losses before the Seller's indemnification obligation is triggered. Once the deductible threshold is exceeded, the Seller is responsible for all losses from the first dollar (a "tipping basket" structure) up to an aggregate cap of $280 million, representing 20 percent of the purchase price. Environmental and tax indemnification obligations under Sections 8.1(b) and 8.1(c) are not subject to the deductible but carry separate caps of $350 million and $70 million, respectively.

The survival period for representations and warranties is 24 months from closing, with the exception of fundamental representations (concerning organization, authority, and title to assets), which survive indefinitely, and environmental representations, which survive for seven years. Tax indemnification obligations survive until 60 days after the expiration of the applicable statute of limitations.

Section 8.5 establishes an escrow arrangement whereby $140 million (10 percent of the purchase price) is deposited into an indemnification escrow account held by a third-party escrow agent. The escrow provides a source of funds for valid indemnification claims and is released to the Seller in three installments: 40 percent at the first anniversary of closing, 40 percent at the second anniversary, and the remaining 20 percent at the third anniversary, subject to retention for pending claims.

## Q1. What type of basket structure is used in the indemnification provisions?
- A. A "true deductible" where the Buyer always bears the first $14 million
- B. No basket or threshold applies to any indemnification claims
- C. A "mini-basket" requiring individual claims to exceed $1 million
- D. A "tipping basket" where the Seller is responsible from the first dollar once the $14 million threshold is exceeded
- Answer: D
- Explanation: "Once the deductible threshold is exceeded, the Seller is responsible for all losses from the first dollar (a 'tipping basket' structure)."

## Q2. Why do environmental representations have a longer survival period than general representations?
- A. Environmental liabilities may take years to discover, requiring extended protection for the Buyer.
- B. Environmental law requires a minimum seven-year warranty period.
- C. The Seller requested a shorter period for environmental claims.
- D. General representations are more important than environmental ones.
- Answer: A
- Explanation: Environmental contamination may not be discovered for years after a transaction, so the seven-year survival (versus 24 months for general reps) provides the Buyer with extended protection against latent environmental issues.

## Q3. What can be inferred about the escrow structure's purpose and design?
- A. It serves solely as a penalty imposed on the Seller for past wrongdoing.
- B. The escrow is funded by the Buyer as a form of insurance premium.
- C. The escrow funds are permanently retained by the Buyer regardless of claims.
- D. It provides security for indemnification claims while gradually returning funds to the Seller as risk diminishes over time.
- Answer: D
- Explanation: The $140 million escrow provides "a source of funds for valid indemnification claims" and is "released to the Seller in three installments" over three years, reflecting decreasing risk over time.

## Q4. The term "fundamental representations" refers to:
- A. The most basic financial projections included in the transaction
- B. Core representations about the Seller's legal existence, authority, and ownership of assets that survive indefinitely
- C. Environmental certifications required by government agencies
- D. Representations about the Buyer's financial capacity to complete the transaction
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Fundamental representations "concerning organization, authority, and title to assets survive indefinitely" — they are the most basic assurances about the Seller's legal standing.
