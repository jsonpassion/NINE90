# Regulatory Framework for Stablecoin Issuance: Implications for Financial Institutions

The enactment of the Digital Asset Stability Act ("DASA") marks a watershed moment in the regulation of stablecoins — digital tokens pegged to fiat currencies or other stable reference assets. The legislation, signed into law in September 2025 with an effective date of July 1, 2026, establishes a comprehensive federal licensing and prudential supervision framework for stablecoin issuers, replacing the fragmented state-level regulatory approach that had previously governed the sector.

Under DASA, any entity issuing stablecoins with an aggregate market capitalization exceeding $5 billion must obtain a federal stablecoin charter from the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency. Chartered issuers are subject to reserve requirements mandating that 100 percent of outstanding stablecoin value be backed by permissible reserve assets, defined as U.S. Treasury securities with maturities not exceeding 90 days, deposits at Federal Reserve member banks, and reverse repurchase agreements collateralized exclusively by Treasury securities. The use of corporate debt, equities, cryptocurrency, or other non-government assets as reserves is explicitly prohibited.

The legislation also addresses the redemption rights of stablecoin holders. Issuers must honor redemption requests at par value within one business day, and must maintain sufficient liquid reserves to process simultaneous redemptions of at least 20 percent of outstanding stablecoin value without liquidating any reserve assets. This liquidity buffer requirement is designed to prevent the bank-run dynamics that contributed to the collapse of several algorithmic stablecoins in previous years.

For traditional financial institutions, DASA presents both opportunities and competitive threats. Banks that obtain stablecoin charters can leverage their existing regulatory infrastructure, compliance capabilities, and customer relationships to establish dominant positions in the stablecoin market. However, they must also contend with the entry of large technology companies that have announced plans to apply for charters, bringing substantial distribution platforms and user bases that could rapidly capture market share.

Industry analysis estimates that the stablecoin market could reach $400 billion in total value within three years of DASA's implementation, with transaction volumes potentially rivaling those of established payment networks. The resulting competitive dynamics between traditional banks, fintech firms, and technology companies will likely reshape the payments landscape fundamentally.

## Q1. What types of assets can be used as reserves under DASA?
- A. Corporate bonds, treasury securities, and cryptocurrency
- B. Real estate, gold, and government securities
- C. Any liquid assets including equities and corporate debt
- D. Only U.S. Treasury securities, Fed member bank deposits, and Treasury-collateralized reverse repos
- Answer: D
- Explanation: Permissible reserves are "U.S. Treasury securities with maturities not exceeding 90 days, deposits at Federal Reserve member banks, and reverse repurchase agreements collateralized exclusively by Treasury securities."

## Q2. What is the purpose of the 20 percent liquidity buffer requirement?
- A. To prevent bank-run dynamics by ensuring issuers can handle large simultaneous redemptions
- B. To generate additional revenue for stablecoin issuers through investments
- C. To limit the total supply of stablecoins in circulation
- D. To provide a reserve for the federal government in case of economic crisis
- Answer: A
- Explanation: The requirement "is designed to prevent the bank-run dynamics that contributed to the collapse of several algorithmic stablecoins."

## Q3. What can be inferred about the competitive landscape after DASA implementation?
- A. Only traditional banks will be permitted to issue stablecoins.
- B. Competition between banks, fintechs, and tech companies for stablecoin market share will intensify significantly.
- C. Technology companies will be prohibited from applying for stablecoin charters.
- D. The stablecoin market will shrink as regulations make issuance unprofitable.
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Banks must "contend with the entry of large technology companies" with "substantial distribution platforms," and the market is projected to reach $400 billion, suggesting intense competition.

## Q4. The phrase "algorithmic stablecoins" that collapsed in previous years most likely refers to:
- A. Tokens that maintained their peg through software mechanisms rather than asset reserves, which proved unstable
- B. Stablecoins backed by physical gold stored in audited vaults
- C. Stablecoins issued by central banks using advanced encryption algorithms
- D. Digital currencies that automatically adjust interest rates based on market conditions
- Answer: A
- Explanation: Context contrasts them with the fully-reserved approach DASA mandates, implying algorithmic stablecoins relied on software/algorithmic mechanisms rather than full reserves, and these proved vulnerable to runs.
