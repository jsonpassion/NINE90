# Memorandum — Transfer Pricing Dispute Resolution: Asia-Pacific Operations

TO: Chief Financial Officer and Global Tax Director
FROM: International Tax Counsel
DATE: December 1, 2025
RE: Status Update on Transfer Pricing Disputes and Recommended Resolution Strategy

This memorandum provides a comprehensive update on the three active transfer pricing disputes affecting our Asia-Pacific operations and recommends a coordinated resolution strategy to minimize aggregate financial exposure and the risk of double taxation.

Dispute 1 — Japan: The National Tax Agency has issued a proposed adjustment of ¥8.4 billion (approximately $56 million) relating to intercompany royalty payments from our Japanese subsidiary to the U.S. parent company for the use of proprietary manufacturing technology and trademarks. The NTA contends that the royalty rate of 5 percent of net sales is excessive relative to arm's length benchmarks, asserting that a rate of 2.5 percent would be appropriate based on comparable uncontrolled transactions identified in its analysis. Our transfer pricing documentation, supported by a detailed functional analysis and a benchmarking study utilizing the comparable uncontrolled price method, supports a rate range of 4.0 to 5.5 percent. We assess the probability of a full adjustment being sustained at 25 percent and a partial adjustment at 55 percent.

Dispute 2 — South Korea: The Korean National Tax Service has challenged the profitability of our Korean distribution subsidiary, asserting that its operating margin of 3.2 percent is below the arm's length range and proposing an adjustment that would increase the subsidiary's taxable income by approximately $28 million over a three-year audit period. Our analysis, applying the transactional net margin method, demonstrates that the subsidiary's margins are consistent with those of comparable independent distributors operating in the Korean market when appropriate adjustments for functional differences are made.

Dispute 3 — India: The Indian tax authorities have issued an order disallowing $18 million in management service fees paid by our Indian subsidiary to the Singapore regional headquarters, characterizing the services as lacking demonstrable benefit to the Indian operations. This characterization fundamentally misapprehends the nature of the services provided, which include strategic planning, treasury management, and IT infrastructure support — all of which provide quantifiable economic value to the Indian subsidiary.

We recommend initiating Mutual Agreement Procedures under the relevant bilateral tax treaties for all three disputes simultaneously, while continuing to contest the adjustments through domestic administrative appeals. This parallel approach maximizes our negotiation leverage and preserves all available remedies.

## Q1. What is the aggregate financial exposure across all three disputes?
- A. Approximately $56 million
- B. Approximately $102 million
- C. Approximately $84 million
- D. Approximately $140 million
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Japan: $56 million + South Korea: $28 million + India: $18 million = $102 million total.

## Q2. What transfer pricing method does the company use to support the Japanese royalty rate?
- A. Transactional net margin method
- B. Profit split method
- C. Comparable uncontrolled price method
- D. Resale price method
- Answer: C
- Explanation: The company's documentation uses "a benchmarking study utilizing the comparable uncontrolled price method."

## Q3. What can be inferred about the recommended dual-track resolution approach?
- A. It strategically preserves all remedies and creates leverage by showing willingness to contest through multiple channels.
- B. It demonstrates weakness by pursuing two approaches simultaneously.
- C. Mutual Agreement Procedures are only available if domestic appeals have been exhausted.
- D. The company has already accepted all proposed adjustments.
- Answer: A
- Explanation: The parallel approach "maximizes our negotiation leverage and preserves all available remedies," showing it is a strategic choice.

## Q4. The phrase "arm's length" in transfer pricing refers to:
- A. The physical distance between related company offices
- B. The principle that transactions between related parties should be priced as if conducted between independent, unrelated parties
- C. A specific measurement of supply chain length for logistics purposes
- D. A regulatory requirement that limits the duration of intercompany contracts
- Answer: B
- Explanation: "Arm's length benchmarks" in transfer pricing describes the standard that intercompany pricing should match what independent parties would agree to in comparable circumstances.
