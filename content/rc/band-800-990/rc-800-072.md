# Climate Transition Risk Assessment

Climate Transition Risk Assessment for the Global Insurance Sector: Regulatory Implications and Balance Sheet Exposure

The insurance sector faces a dual climate transition challenge that distinguishes it from most other financial institutions. As both underwriters of climate-related risks and investors in carbon-intensive assets, insurers are exposed on both sides of the balance sheet to the economic realignment driven by the transition to a low-carbon economy. This assessment quantifies these exposures and evaluates the regulatory trajectory that is accelerating the industry's need to integrate climate considerations into core business decisions.

On the underwriting side, the transition to clean energy and the phase-out of fossil fuel infrastructure create emerging liability risks. Policies written for coal-fired power plants, petroleum refineries, and associated transportation infrastructure face declining premium pools as these assets are retired or repurposed. The Geneva Association estimates that global non-life insurance premiums associated with fossil fuel assets will contract by 35 to 45 percent by 2035 under moderate transition scenarios. Simultaneously, new categories of transition-related claims are emerging, including directors' and officers' liability claims alleging inadequate climate risk disclosure, professional indemnity claims against consultants who provided sustainability assessments later found to be deficient, and product liability claims related to the environmental performance of products marketed as sustainable.

The investment side presents equal if not greater challenges. Analysis of the aggregate investment portfolios of the 50 largest global insurers reveals that approximately 12 percent of total invested assets, representing an estimated $2.1 trillion, are in sectors classified as highly exposed to transition risk under the Network for Greening the Financial System taxonomy. The potential for stranded assets — investments that lose value prematurely due to the transition — is particularly acute in the fixed income portfolios that dominate insurance company asset allocations, where long duration bonds issued by carbon-intensive companies face the dual risk of credit deterioration and forced early sale as insurers come under pressure to decarbonize their portfolios.

Regulatory momentum is intensifying. The International Association of Insurance Supervisors has published guidance recommending that supervisors require insurers to conduct climate scenario analysis as part of their Own Risk and Solvency Assessment processes. The European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority has gone further, proposing that climate transition risk be incorporated into the standard formula for calculating solvency capital requirements, which would directly increase the capital charges associated with carbon-intensive investments. If adopted, this proposal would effectively create a regulatory cost of approximately 15 to 25 basis points on insurer returns from fossil-fuel-related investments, potentially triggering a significant reallocation of capital.

## Q1. Why is the insurance sector uniquely exposed to climate transition risk?
- A. It is the only sector subject to climate-related regulations.
- B. It faces exposure on both sides of its balance sheet — as underwriters and investors.
- C. It is exclusively invested in fossil fuel assets.
- D. It lacks the financial expertise to model climate scenarios.
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Insurers are 'exposed on both sides of the balance sheet' as 'both underwriters of climate-related risks and investors in carbon-intensive assets.'
