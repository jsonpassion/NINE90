# Pharmaceutical Supply Chain Resilience: Lessons from Recent Disruptions

A comprehensive industry study conducted by the Global Pharmaceutical Research Consortium has highlighted critical vulnerabilities in the international pharmaceutical supply chain, drawing on data from disruption events over the past three years. The study, which surveyed 180 pharmaceutical manufacturers, distributors, and healthcare providers across 22 countries, identifies concentration risk as the most significant systemic threat to drug availability.

According to the findings, approximately 70 percent of active pharmaceutical ingredients used in generic medications worldwide are manufactured in just three countries: India, China, and Ireland. This geographic concentration means that a single event, whether a natural disaster, regulatory action, or geopolitical conflict, can trigger cascading shortages affecting patients in dozens of countries simultaneously. The study documents 47 significant drug shortage events during the review period, of which 32 were directly attributable to supply chain concentration.

The consortium's recommendations center on three strategic pillars. First, governments should incentivize the diversification of manufacturing locations through tax credits and expedited regulatory approvals for new production facilities. Second, pharmaceutical companies should maintain strategic reserves of critical medications equivalent to at least 90 days of domestic consumption, up from the current average of approximately 45 days. Third, the industry should invest in advanced supply chain visibility platforms that enable real-time tracking of raw materials and finished products across all tiers of the supply chain.

The study also emphasizes the importance of international cooperation, noting that unilateral export restrictions imposed by several countries during recent crises exacerbated shortages elsewhere. The consortium advocates for the establishment of a multilateral pharmaceutical supply accord that would commit signatory nations to maintaining open trade in essential medicines during emergencies.
