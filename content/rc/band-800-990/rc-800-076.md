# Cross-Border M&A Due Diligence

Cross-Border Mergers and Acquisitions: Anti-Corruption Due Diligence in Jurisdictions with Elevated Compliance Risk

The acquisition of companies with operations in jurisdictions classified as elevated compliance risk under the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index requires a substantially enhanced due diligence protocol that extends well beyond the standard financial, legal, and operational review. This memorandum establishes the minimum requirements for anti-corruption due diligence in such transactions, informed by the enforcement priorities of the US Department of Justice, the UK Serious Fraud Office, and the French Parquet National Financier, all of which have demonstrated a willingness to hold acquiring companies liable for pre-acquisition corrupt conduct by target companies under successor liability doctrines.

The cornerstone of enhanced due diligence is the comprehensive review of the target company's relationships with government officials and state-owned enterprises. This review must encompass all contracts with government entities or state-owned enterprises, including subcontracts; all payments to government-affiliated consultants, agents, and intermediaries; political contributions and lobbying expenditures; charitable donations to organizations associated with government officials; and hospitality and travel expenses involving government officials exceeding $500 in aggregate per individual per year. Red flags identified during this review include payments to intermediaries in jurisdictions different from where the services were performed, commissions significantly exceeding market rates for comparable services, and the use of shell companies or nominee arrangements that obscure the ultimate beneficial ownership of the recipient.

The due diligence team must also evaluate the adequacy of the target's existing compliance program against the standards articulated in the DOJ's Evaluation of Corporate Compliance Programs guidance. Key assessment criteria include the degree to which anti-corruption policies are embedded in operational processes rather than existing merely as written policies, the independence and resourcing of the compliance function, the comprehensiveness of training programs with a focus on high-risk employees, the effectiveness of internal reporting channels, and the consistency of disciplinary measures for compliance violations across all levels of the organization.

Where due diligence identifies material compliance deficiencies or suspicious transactions, the acquisition agreement should include specific protective provisions: enhanced representations and warranties regarding compliance with applicable anti-corruption laws; an indemnification clause requiring the sellers to bear financial responsibility for any liabilities arising from pre-acquisition conduct; a purchase price adjustment mechanism triggered by post-closing discovery of previously undisclosed compliance issues; and, in transactions structured as asset acquisitions, explicit exclusion of liabilities associated with contracts identified as high-risk during the due diligence process.

The estimated cost of a comprehensive anti-corruption due diligence program for a mid-sized cross-border acquisition ranges from $1.5 million to $4 million, depending on the complexity of the target's operations and the number of high-risk jurisdictions involved. This investment must be weighed against the potential costs of enforcement actions, which in recent cases have resulted in combined penalties exceeding $500 million.

## Q1. What liability risk do acquiring companies face?
- A. Tax liability for the target's unpaid obligations
- B. Successor liability for pre-acquisition corrupt conduct by the target company
- C. Personal liability for the target's employees
- D. Environmental cleanup costs for the target's operations
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Enforcement authorities have 'demonstrated a willingness to hold acquiring companies liable for pre-acquisition corrupt conduct under successor liability doctrines.'

## Q2. Which of the following is identified as a red flag during due diligence?
- A. Regular audits by external accounting firms
- B. Payments to intermediaries in jurisdictions different from where services were performed
- C. Charitable donations to established international organizations
- D. Employment of in-house legal counsel in the target company
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Red flags include 'payments to intermediaries in jurisdictions different from where the services were performed.'

## Q3. What protective provision should the acquisition agreement include?
- A. A non-compete clause for the target's management team
- B. A mandatory post-acquisition audit by external regulators
- C. An indemnification clause requiring sellers to bear financial responsibility for pre-acquisition compliance liabilities
- D. A requirement that all target employees pass background checks
- Answer: C
- Explanation: The agreement should include 'an indemnification clause requiring the sellers to bear financial responsibility for any liabilities arising from pre-acquisition conduct.'

## Q4. How does the cost of due diligence compare to potential enforcement penalties?
- A. Due diligence costs $1.5 to $4 million, while enforcement actions have exceeded $500 million.
- B. Due diligence and enforcement costs are approximately equal.
- C. Due diligence typically costs more than the resulting penalties.
- D. Enforcement penalties are capped at $10 million regardless of the offense.
- Answer: A
- Explanation: Due diligence costs '$1.5 million to $4 million' while enforcement actions 'have resulted in combined penalties exceeding $500 million.'
