# Annual Report — Kensington Global Asset Management

Kensington Global Asset Management reports that assets under management ("AUM") reached $248 billion as of September 30, 2025, representing a 12 percent increase from $221 billion at the end of the prior fiscal year. The growth was driven by $18 billion in net client inflows and $9 billion in market appreciation, partially offset by $1.4 billion in fee-related distributions and miscellaneous outflows.

Revenue for the fiscal year totaled $2.18 billion, an increase of 15 percent over the prior year. Management fees, which constitute the most stable component of revenue, grew 11 percent to $1.62 billion, reflecting the growth in average AUM and a modest improvement in the blended fee rate from 69 to 71 basis points. Performance fees contributed $420 million, more than double the prior year's $185 million, driven by strong absolute and relative returns in the firm's equity and multi-strategy hedge fund products during a year of elevated market volatility.

The firm's fee mix evolution warrants attention. Performance fees now represent 19 percent of total revenue, up from 10 percent in the prior year. While performance fees are inherently more volatile than management fees, the increase reflects the growing proportion of AUM in higher-fee alternative investment strategies, which have expanded from 22 percent to 28 percent of total AUM. This strategic shift toward alternatives is deliberate, as these products command significantly higher fee rates — averaging 145 basis points compared with 52 basis points for traditional strategies — and are less susceptible to the secular fee compression affecting the traditional asset management industry.

Operating margin expanded to 38 percent from 34 percent, reflecting the positive operating leverage inherent in the asset management business model, where incremental AUM generates revenue with minimal additional cost. Adjusted earnings per share grew 22 percent to $4.86.

The firm declared a special distribution of $2.00 per share in addition to the regular quarterly dividend of $0.45 per share, reflecting the strong performance fee generation during the year. The Board noted that special distributions should not be expected annually and will be evaluated based on the firm's performance fee realization and capital requirements.

## Q1. What drove the increase in performance fee revenue?
- A. A reduction in management fees that was offset by performance fees
- B. Regulatory changes that mandated higher performance fees
- C. Strong returns in equity and hedge fund products during elevated market volatility
- D. The firm's decision to eliminate management fees entirely
- Answer: C
- Explanation: Performance fees were "driven by strong absolute and relative returns in the firm's equity and multi-strategy hedge fund products during a year of elevated market volatility."

## Q2. Why is Kensington strategically shifting toward alternative investment products?
- A. Regulators require all asset managers to offer alternative products.
- B. Client demand for alternative products has declined, creating a contrarian opportunity.
- C. Traditional investment strategies have been banned by new regulations.
- D. Alternative strategies command higher fees and are less affected by industry-wide fee compression.
- Answer: D
- Explanation: Alternatives "command significantly higher fee rates — averaging 145 basis points compared with 52 basis points — and are less susceptible to the secular fee compression."

## Q3. What can be inferred about the sustainability of the special distribution?
- A. The Board expects to pay the same special distribution every year going forward.
- B. Special distributions will increase by 10 percent each year.
- C. The Board explicitly cautioned that special distributions are not guaranteed annually and depend on performance.
- D. The special distribution replaced the regular quarterly dividend permanently.
- Answer: C
- Explanation: "The Board noted that special distributions should not be expected annually and will be evaluated based on the firm's performance fee realization and capital requirements."

## Q4. The phrase "operating leverage" in the asset management context refers to:
- A. The use of financial debt to amplify investment returns
- B. The ability to grow revenue from additional AUM without proportionally increasing operating costs
- C. A mechanical advantage gained by using specialized trading equipment
- D. The regulatory leverage that larger firms have over smaller competitors
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Operating leverage describes how "incremental AUM generates revenue with minimal additional cost," causing margins to expand as the firm scales.
