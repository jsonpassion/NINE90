# Annual Letter to Shareholders — Meridian Diversified Holdings

Dear Shareholders,

Our philosophy at Meridian has always been grounded in the conviction that disciplined capital allocation, executed over extended time horizons, is the primary determinant of long-term shareholder value creation. This year's results provide both validation of that philosophy and a reminder that the path to long-term value is not always linear.

Meridian's intrinsic value per share, which we estimate using a sum-of-the-parts methodology based on the discounted future cash flows of our operating subsidiaries and the market values of our investment portfolio, increased approximately 11 percent during 2025 to an estimated $342 per share. This compares with a 7 percent increase in our publicly traded share price, suggesting that the market discount to intrinsic value widened during the year — a situation we have observed before and which we believe represents a long-term opportunity for patient investors.

Our insurance operations generated combined underwriting profit of $480 million, benefiting from favorable loss experience and the disciplined pricing actions taken during the past two renewal cycles. The insurance float — premiums received in advance of claims payment — grew to $24.8 billion, providing us with a substantial pool of investable capital at a negative cost, given that our underwriting operations are profitable. This float is the foundation of our investment program and has been the single most important factor in Meridian's long-term compounding record.

Among our wholly owned operating companies, the most notable development was the acquisition of Hartfield Precision Manufacturing for $920 million. Hartfield generates $145 million in pre-tax earnings and operates in a niche industrial market with significant barriers to entry. We paid approximately 6.3 times pre-tax earnings, a valuation we consider attractive for a business of this quality and competitive positioning. The acquisition was funded entirely from available cash, and we retain more than $32 billion in cash and short-term investments for future deployment.

Our investment portfolio returned 9.4 percent during the year, with our concentrated equity positions in five companies accounting for 78 percent of the portfolio's total value. We added no new positions during the year and made no significant changes to existing positions, consistent with our preference for long-term ownership of exceptional businesses at sensible prices.

## Q1. What is the estimated intrinsic value per share of Meridian?
- A. $280
- B. $311
- C. $342
- D. $380
- Answer: C
- Explanation: "Intrinsic value per share increased approximately 11 percent during 2025 to an estimated $342 per share."

## Q2. Why is insurance float described as having a "negative cost"?
- A. The insurance operations are losing money, making the float expensive.
- B. The government subsidizes the cost of maintaining the float.
- C. Insurance premiums are refunded to policyholders at year-end.
- D. The float is obtained at no cost because the insurance operations that generate it are profitable.
- Answer: D
- Explanation: Float has "a negative cost, given that our underwriting operations are profitable" — the company earns money from the insurance business itself while also investing the premiums.

## Q3. What can be inferred about Meridian's investment style?
- A. The company actively trades hundreds of stocks to maximize short-term returns.
- B. The company invests exclusively in government bonds.
- C. Investment decisions are made by an external fund manager.
- D. The company maintains a highly concentrated portfolio with a long-term ownership orientation.
- Answer: D
- Explanation: Five positions account for 78 percent of portfolio value, no positions were added, and no significant changes were made — indicating concentrated, long-term investment.

## Q4. The phrase "sum-of-the-parts methodology" for estimating intrinsic value means:
- A. Using only the stock market price to determine the company's worth
- B. Dividing the total share price by the number of business divisions
- C. Calculating value by adding up the individual estimated values of each business unit and asset
- D. Averaging the valuations provided by multiple investment banks
- Answer: C
- Explanation: Sum-of-the-parts values a conglomerate by independently valuing each subsidiary ("discounted future cash flows of our operating subsidiaries") and investment holding, then adding them together.
