# Memorandum — Impact Assessment: OECD Global Minimum Tax Implementation

TO: Board of Directors and Finance Committee
FROM: Global Tax Strategy Group
DATE: November 20, 2025
RE: Financial Impact of Pillar Two Global Minimum Tax on Consolidated Group Operations

This memorandum assesses the financial impact of the OECD/G20 Inclusive Framework's Pillar Two Global Minimum Tax ("GloBE Rules"), which establishes a minimum effective tax rate of 15 percent for multinational enterprises with consolidated revenue exceeding €750 million. With 142 jurisdictions having agreed to the framework and 38 having enacted implementing legislation effective January 1, 2025, compliance is now an operational reality for our consolidated group.

Our preliminary impact analysis indicates that the GloBE Rules will increase our consolidated effective tax rate from the current 18.4 percent to approximately 20.1 percent, representing an incremental annual tax liability of approximately $92 million. The increase is attributable to top-up taxes that will be payable in respect of three jurisdictions where our current effective tax rates fall below the 15 percent minimum: Singapore (current ETR 8.2 percent), Ireland (current ETR 11.8 percent), and the United Arab Emirates (current ETR 2.4 percent).

The most significant impact arises from our Singapore operations, which account for approximately $1.8 billion in revenue and currently benefit from a concessionary tax rate under the Economic Expansion Incentives Act. The top-up tax on Singapore profits is estimated at $58 million annually. The Irish operations, which house our European intellectual property holding structure, would incur an estimated top-up of $22 million, and UAE operations would contribute approximately $12 million.

Our analysis has considered several potential mitigation strategies. First, restructuring of intragroup royalty flows to reduce the profit allocated to low-tax jurisdictions may provide partial relief, though the GloBE Rules' Substance-Based Income Exclusion limits the effectiveness of pure profit-shifting strategies by providing carve-outs only for genuine economic activity, calculated as 5 percent of eligible payroll costs and tangible asset book values in each jurisdiction. Second, acceleration of capital expenditures in affected jurisdictions could increase the Substance-Based Income Exclusion and reduce the top-up tax exposure, though this approach carries its own investment risks and must be justified on commercial grounds independent of tax considerations.

We recommend that the Finance Committee authorize a comprehensive review of the group's operating model and intellectual property ownership structure to identify optimization opportunities consistent with the GloBE Rules' substance requirements.

## Q1. What is the estimated annual increase in tax liability under the GloBE Rules?
- A. $22 million
- B. $58 million
- C. $92 million
- D. $142 million
- Answer: C
- Explanation: "The GloBE Rules will increase our consolidated effective tax rate, representing an incremental annual tax liability of approximately $92 million."

## Q2. Which jurisdiction contributes the largest top-up tax exposure?
- A. Ireland
- B. Singapore
- C. The United States
- D. United Arab Emirates
- Answer: B
- Explanation: "The most significant impact arises from our Singapore operations" with "top-up tax estimated at $58 million annually."

## Q3. What can be inferred about the effectiveness of traditional profit-shifting strategies under the GloBE Rules?
- A. They remain fully effective and can eliminate all top-up tax obligations.
- B. They are more effective under the new rules than under previous tax frameworks.
- C. They are explicitly prohibited and would result in criminal penalties.
- D. Their effectiveness is significantly limited by substance requirements that tie tax benefits to real economic activity.
- Answer: D
- Explanation: The Substance-Based Income Exclusion "limits the effectiveness of pure profit-shifting strategies by providing carve-outs only for genuine economic activity."

## Q4. The phrase "Substance-Based Income Exclusion" refers to:
- A. A mechanism that reduces top-up tax exposure based on actual economic activity (payroll and assets) in each jurisdiction
- B. A rule that excludes all income from taxation in low-tax jurisdictions
- C. An exemption for companies with fewer than 750 employees
- D. A provision allowing companies to exclude losses from the minimum tax calculation
- Answer: A
- Explanation: The exclusion is "calculated as 5 percent of eligible payroll costs and tangible asset book values in each jurisdiction," providing relief based on genuine economic substance.
