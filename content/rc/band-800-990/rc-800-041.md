# SEC Finalizes Climate Disclosure Rules for Public Companies

The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission has finalized its long-anticipated climate-related disclosure rules, establishing mandatory reporting requirements for publicly traded companies beginning with fiscal years starting after January 1, 2026. The rules, adopted by a 3-2 vote along party lines, represent the most significant expansion of corporate disclosure requirements since the implementation of Sarbanes-Oxley in 2002.

Under the final rules, large accelerated filers must disclose Scope 1 and Scope 2 greenhouse gas emissions, subject to third-party attestation beginning in the second year of reporting. Scope 3 emissions disclosure, which encompasses indirect emissions from a company's value chain, was included in the proposed rules but was removed from the final version due to concerns about measurement reliability and compliance costs. However, companies that have made public commitments or set targets involving Scope 3 emissions must disclose those commitments and their progress toward achieving them.

The rules also require disclosure of climate-related risks that have materially affected or are reasonably likely to affect a company's business strategy, results of operations, or financial condition. Companies must describe any processes they have established for identifying, assessing, and managing material climate risks, as well as the governance mechanisms through which the board of directors oversees climate-related risks. Financial statement disclosures must quantify the impact of severe weather events and other natural conditions on individual line items when such impacts exceed one percent of the relevant financial statement line item.

The business community's response has been divided. Supporters argue that standardized climate disclosures will improve market efficiency by providing investors with comparable, decision-useful information about material climate risks. Critics contend that the rules impose substantial compliance costs — estimated by the SEC at $640,000 to $1.2 million annually for large filers — without providing proportionate benefits, and that existing securities fraud liability provisions will deter companies from making forward-looking climate-related statements.

Multiple legal challenges have been filed, primarily asserting that the SEC has exceeded its statutory authority by mandating disclosures that extend beyond traditional financial materiality. The outcome of these challenges will likely be determined by the Supreme Court.

## Q1. Why was Scope 3 emissions disclosure removed from the final rules?
- A. Scope 3 emissions are considered irrelevant by all investors.
- B. Concerns about measurement reliability and compliance costs led to its removal.
- C. International regulations already require Scope 3 disclosure.
- D. The SEC determined that no companies produce Scope 3 emissions.
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Scope 3 "was removed from the final version due to concerns about measurement reliability and compliance costs."

## Q2. When must the impact of weather events be disclosed in financial statements?
- A. Only when the company reports a net loss for the fiscal year
- B. When the impact exceeds one percent of the relevant financial statement line item
- C. For all weather events regardless of financial impact
- D. Only when the weather event causes physical damage to company property
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Companies must disclose "when such impacts exceed one percent of the relevant financial statement line item."

## Q3. What can be inferred about the political nature of climate disclosure regulation?
- A. The rules were adopted with bipartisan consensus and universal support.
- B. Climate disclosure is a noncontroversial technical accounting matter.
- C. The 3-2 party-line vote and subsequent legal challenges indicate significant political and ideological division.
- D. All business groups and companies oppose the rules without exception.
- Answer: C
- Explanation: The 3-2 party-line vote, divided business response, and multiple legal challenges all indicate significant political division.

## Q4. The phrase "decision-useful information" in the supporters' argument means:
- A. Information that helps regulators decide which companies to investigate
- B. Data that investors can effectively use to make informed investment and capital allocation decisions
- C. Reports that help companies decide which products to develop
- D. Statistics that allow the government to make policy decisions about climate change
- Answer: B
- Explanation: In the context of investor disclosure, "decision-useful" means information that helps investors evaluate risks and make informed investment decisions.
