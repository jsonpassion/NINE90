# Semiconductor Industry Outlook: Capital Expenditure Cycle and Supply-Demand Dynamics

The global semiconductor industry is navigating a complex inflection point as massive capital expenditure commitments made during the capacity shortage of 2021-2023 begin to yield new production capacity while demand growth in certain end markets decelerates. Industry capital expenditure is projected to reach $198 billion in 2025, the third consecutive year above $180 billion, creating the potential for localized oversupply in certain device categories even as structural shortages persist in others.

The memory semiconductor segment illustrates this divergence. DRAM production capacity has expanded by 24 percent over the past two years, exceeding demand growth of 18 percent and contributing to a 15 percent decline in average selling prices during the first half of 2025. However, the emerging demand for high-bandwidth memory ("HBM") used in artificial intelligence accelerators has created a parallel shortage, with HBM prices increasing 35 percent year-over-year and major AI chipmakers securing multi-year supply agreements at premium prices.

The leading-edge logic segment presents a different dynamic. Fabrication capacity for advanced nodes below 5 nanometers remains constrained, with utilization rates exceeding 95 percent at the three foundries capable of producing these devices. The concentration of advanced manufacturing capability represents both a competitive moat for the leading foundries and a geopolitical vulnerability that has prompted government interventions, including $52 billion in U.S. CHIPS Act funding and €43 billion in the European Chips Act, aimed at diversifying the geographic distribution of semiconductor manufacturing.

Inventory dynamics across the supply chain are sending mixed signals. Chip inventory at major electronic device manufacturers has risen to 68 days of supply, above the five-year average of 58 days, suggesting potential order cutbacks in the near term. However, automotive and industrial semiconductor inventories remain below normalized levels at 42 days, reflecting the longer design cycles and more conservative inventory practices in these end markets.

Industry forecasters project overall semiconductor revenue growth of 8 to 12 percent in 2026, supported by the structural demand tailwinds from AI infrastructure deployment, automotive electrification, and industrial automation, tempered by the cyclical headwinds of elevated inventories in consumer electronics.

## Q1. What is creating divergent pricing trends within the memory segment?
- A. All memory types are experiencing identical price declines.
- B. Oversupply in standard DRAM is depressing prices while AI-driven demand for HBM creates shortages and premium pricing.
- C. Government regulations are setting memory chip prices in all markets.
- D. Memory chip demand has collapsed entirely across all categories.
- Answer: B
- Explanation: DRAM prices fell 15 percent due to capacity exceeding demand, while HBM prices rose 35 percent driven by AI accelerator demand — a clear divergence.

## Q2. Why have governments invested heavily in semiconductor manufacturing?
- A. The concentration of advanced manufacturing in few locations creates a geopolitical vulnerability that governments want to diversify.
- B. To create jobs in the construction industry exclusively
- C. Government agencies need semiconductors exclusively for military applications.
- D. Private companies have ceased all investment in semiconductor manufacturing.
- Answer: A
- Explanation: The concentration of advanced capability "represents a geopolitical vulnerability that has prompted government interventions" including CHIPS Act funding "aimed at diversifying the geographic distribution."

## Q3. What can be inferred about near-term semiconductor order trends?
- A. Orders will increase dramatically across all segments.
- B. All semiconductor orders will decline to zero within the next quarter.
- C. Consumer electronics segments may see order cutbacks while automotive and industrial demand remains strong.
- D. Only memory chip orders will be affected by inventory levels.
- Answer: C
- Explanation: Electronic device manufacturer inventory above average (68 vs 58 days) "suggesting potential order cutbacks" while automotive/industrial remain below normalized levels, indicating continued ordering.

## Q4. The term "utilization rate" in the context of semiconductor fabrication refers to:
- A. The proportion of a factory's maximum production capacity that is actively being used
- B. The percentage of employees working at a facility at any given time
- C. The share of total semiconductor revenue earned by a single company
- D. The ratio of successful chips to defective chips produced
- Answer: A
- Explanation: A utilization rate "exceeding 95 percent" at foundries means nearly all available production capacity is in use, indicating strong demand relative to supply.
