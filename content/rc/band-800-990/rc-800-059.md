# Legal Memorandum — Fiduciary Duty Considerations in Stakeholder-Oriented Decision Making

TO: Board of Directors
FROM: General Counsel
DATE: December 10, 2025
RE: Legal Framework for Incorporating Stakeholder Interests in Board Decision Making

This memorandum addresses the legal boundaries within which the Board of Directors may consider the interests of non-shareholder stakeholders — including employees, communities, customers, and the environment — in making governance decisions. The analysis is prompted by the Board's upcoming consideration of a proposed facility closure that would generate estimated annual savings of $45 million but would result in the elimination of 620 positions in a community where the company is the largest employer.

Under traditional Delaware corporate law, which governs our corporation, directors owe fiduciary duties of care and loyalty to the corporation and its shareholders. The landmark Revlon, Inc. v. MacAndrews & Forbes Holdings decision established that when a company is being sold or broken up, the board's sole obligation is to maximize shareholder value. However, outside the Revlon context, the business judgment rule provides directors with substantial discretion in determining how best to promote the long-term interests of the corporation.

The Delaware Supreme Court's decision in Unocal Corp. v. Mesa Petroleum and subsequent jurisprudence have recognized that directors may consider the interests of constituencies other than shareholders when such consideration is rationally related to the long-term value of the corporation. This principle permits — but does not require — the Board to weigh factors such as the impact of decisions on employee morale and retention, community relations and the company's social license to operate, long-term reputational effects that may influence customer behavior and talent acquisition, and regulatory and political risks that could affect the company's operating environment.

The critical legal requirement is that any decision to moderate near-term shareholder returns in favor of stakeholder considerations must be supportable by a rational connection to long-term corporate value. The Board cannot subordinate shareholder interests to stakeholder interests as an end in itself; rather, it must articulate how the consideration of stakeholder interests serves the long-term economic interests of the corporation and its shareholders.

In the context of the proposed facility closure, this framework permits the Board to consider alternatives such as phased closure, severance and retraining programs, community transition support, or partial closure combined with operational restructuring — provided that the chosen approach can be justified as serving the corporation's long-term interests, even if it results in less than the maximum possible near-term cost savings.

## Q1. Under what circumstances must a board focus exclusively on maximizing shareholder value?
- A. In all business decisions at all times without exception
- B. When the company's stock price falls below a certain threshold
- C. Only when shareholder activists file formal demands
- D. When the company is being sold or broken up, as established in the Revlon decision
- Answer: D
- Explanation: "The Revlon decision established that when a company is being sold or broken up, the board's sole obligation is to maximize shareholder value."

## Q2. What is the "rational connection" requirement for stakeholder-oriented decisions?
- A. The board must demonstrate that considering stakeholder interests ultimately serves the corporation's long-term economic interests.
- B. Decisions favoring stakeholders must be approved by a unanimous shareholder vote.
- C. A mathematical formula must prove that stakeholder costs equal shareholder benefits.
- D. External consultants must certify that the decision is rational.
- Answer: A
- Explanation: "Any decision to moderate near-term shareholder returns in favor of stakeholder considerations must be supportable by a rational connection to long-term corporate value."

## Q3. What can be inferred about the legal evolution of corporate purpose?
- A. Corporate law has always required equal treatment of all stakeholders.
- B. Only non-profit corporations may consider stakeholder interests.
- C. Courts have completely abandoned the concept of shareholder primacy.
- D. The law is gradually recognizing broader considerations while maintaining shareholder primacy as the ultimate standard.
- Answer: D
- Explanation: Courts "have recognized that directors may consider the interests of constituencies other than shareholders" but "cannot subordinate shareholder interests to stakeholder interests as an end in itself."

## Q4. The phrase "social license to operate" in this context refers to:
- A. A government permit required to operate business facilities
- B. A contractual agreement between the company and local government
- C. The community acceptance and goodwill that enables a company to conduct its business without significant opposition
- D. The company's status as a registered social enterprise
- Answer: C
- Explanation: In the context of community relations and reputational effects, "social license to operate" refers to the informal acceptance by a community that allows a company to function without significant opposition or disruption.
