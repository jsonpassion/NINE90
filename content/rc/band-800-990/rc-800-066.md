# Data Privacy Regulatory Compliance Framework

ENTERPRISE DATA PRIVACY COMPLIANCE FRAMEWORK — REGULATORY ALIGNMENT AND OPERATIONAL IMPLEMENTATION

Prepared by the Office of the Chief Privacy Officer for the Executive Committee

This document establishes the enterprise-wide framework governing the collection, processing, storage, and cross-border transfer of personal data, designed to ensure compliance with the overlapping and, in certain instances, conflicting requirements of the European Union General Data Protection Regulation ("GDPR"), the California Consumer Privacy Act as amended by the California Privacy Rights Act ("CCPA/CPRA"), and the recently enacted Federal Digital Privacy Act of 2025 ("FDPA"). The framework supersedes all previously issued data privacy policies and is binding upon all business units, subsidiaries, and third-party processors acting on behalf of the organization.

The foundational principle of this framework is data minimization — the restriction of personal data collection to information that is demonstrably necessary for a specified, legitimate business purpose. Each business unit must maintain a current data inventory documenting the categories of personal data collected, the specific purposes for which each category is processed, the legal basis for processing under each applicable jurisdiction, the retention period, and the identity of any third parties with whom the data is shared. Data inventories must be reviewed and certified by the business unit's designated privacy steward on a quarterly basis.

Cross-border data transfers present the most legally complex aspect of the framework. Following the invalidation of the EU-US Privacy Shield and the subsequent adoption of the EU-US Data Privacy Framework, all transfers of personal data from the European Economic Area to the United States must rely on Standard Contractual Clauses supplemented by a Transfer Impact Assessment documenting that the legal regime in the destination country provides substantially equivalent protections. The Transfer Impact Assessment must evaluate surveillance laws, government access provisions, and available legal remedies, and must be reviewed annually or whenever there is a material change in the relevant legal environment.

Breach notification obligations vary by jurisdiction and must be managed through a centralized Incident Response Protocol. Under the GDPR, supervisory authorities must be notified within 72 hours of becoming aware of a breach involving personal data. The FDPA requires notification to affected individuals within 60 days. The CCPA/CPRA provides for a private right of action for unauthorized access resulting from a business's failure to implement reasonable security measures, creating potential class action exposure. The Incident Response Protocol establishes a maximum internal escalation time of four hours from initial detection to notification of the Chief Privacy Officer, ensuring sufficient time for investigation, impact assessment, and preparation of regulatory notifications within the applicable deadlines.

Non-compliance penalties under these regulatory regimes are substantial: up to 4 percent of global annual turnover under the GDPR, $7,500 per intentional violation under the CCPA/CPRA, and up to $50,000 per violation under the FDPA, with the possibility of multiplicative penalties for patterns of non-compliance.

## Q1. What foundational principle governs the data privacy framework?
- A. Data maximization for comprehensive customer profiling
- B. Data minimization — restricting collection to what is demonstrably necessary
- C. Data monetization through third-party sharing arrangements
- D. Data centralization in a single global repository
- Answer: B
- Explanation: The framework's foundational principle is 'data minimization — the restriction of personal data collection to information that is demonstrably necessary for a specified, legitimate business purpose.'

## Q2. What must supplement Standard Contractual Clauses for EU-to-US data transfers?
- A. An executive certification from the CEO
- B. A Transfer Impact Assessment evaluating surveillance laws and government access provisions
- C. A binding arbitration agreement with EU regulators
- D. An insurance policy covering potential data breach losses
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Transfers must rely on Standard Contractual Clauses 'supplemented by a Transfer Impact Assessment documenting that the legal regime provides substantially equivalent protections.'

## Q3. What unique legal risk does the CCPA/CPRA create?
- A. Criminal prosecution of corporate officers
- B. Mandatory data deletion within 24 hours of a breach
- C. A private right of action for unauthorized access, creating potential class action exposure
- D. Automatic revocation of business licenses
- Answer: C
- Explanation: The CCPA/CPRA 'provides for a private right of action for unauthorized access resulting from a business's failure to implement reasonable security measures, creating potential class action exposure.'

## Q4. What is the maximum GDPR penalty described?
- A. $50,000 per violation
- B. $7,500 per intentional violation
- C. Up to 4 percent of global annual turnover
- D. A fixed fine of $10 million
- Answer: C
- Explanation: GDPR penalties can reach 'up to 4 percent of global annual turnover.'
