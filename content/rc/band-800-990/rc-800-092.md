# The Economics of Cloud Migration: Calculating Total Cost of Ownership

As enterprises continue to evaluate the financial implications of migrating their IT infrastructure to cloud platforms, a detailed study by the Enterprise Technology Research Consortium challenges the widespread assumption that cloud computing always delivers cost savings relative to on-premises systems. The study, based on a five-year total cost of ownership analysis of 150 enterprise migrations, reveals a more nuanced picture that depends critically on workload characteristics, organizational readiness, and migration strategy execution.

The research found that organizations with variable or unpredictable workloads achieved an average cost reduction of 28 percent over five years after migrating to the cloud, primarily due to the ability to scale computing resources dynamically and avoid the capital expenditure associated with provisioning for peak demand. Conversely, organizations with stable, predictable workloads experienced an average cost increase of 12 percent, as the ongoing operational expenses of cloud services exceeded the amortized cost of owning and maintaining dedicated hardware.

A critical variable that the study identifies is the concept of "cloud waste," defined as spending on cloud resources that are provisioned but not actively utilized. The average enterprise in the study allocated 35 percent of its cloud budget to underutilized or idle resources, with the most common sources of waste being oversized virtual machines, unused storage volumes, and non-production environments left running outside of business hours. Organizations that implemented automated resource optimization tools reduced their cloud waste by an average of 60 percent, resulting in significant overall cost improvements.

The consortium recommends that organizations conduct detailed workload assessments before initiating migration, establish dedicated cloud financial management teams to monitor and optimize spending continuously, and consider hybrid architectures that place stable workloads on owned infrastructure while leveraging cloud platforms for variable and experimental workloads. The report concludes that cloud migration should be viewed not as an inherently cost-saving measure, but as a strategic capability investment that requires deliberate financial management to deliver its full value.
