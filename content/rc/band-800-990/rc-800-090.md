# Sustainable Aviation Fuels: Progress and Barriers to Widespread Adoption

The aviation industry's pursuit of carbon neutrality has placed sustainable aviation fuels at the forefront of decarbonization strategies, yet significant technical, economic, and regulatory hurdles continue to impede their large-scale adoption. A comprehensive analysis published by the Aviation Sustainability Research Foundation examines the current state of SAF production and deployment, identifying both promising developments and persistent challenges that must be addressed to meet the industry's ambitious emissions reduction targets.

Global SAF production capacity has tripled over the past three years, reaching approximately 600 million liters annually. While this growth is noteworthy, it represents less than 0.2 percent of the 300 billion liters of conventional jet fuel consumed each year, illustrating the enormous gap between current supply and the volumes required to achieve meaningful emissions reductions. The International Air Transport Association has set a target of 10 percent SAF utilization by 2030, which would require annual production to increase to roughly 30 billion liters, a fifty-fold expansion from current levels.

The primary barrier to scaling SAF production remains cost. Current SAF prices range from two to four times the cost of conventional jet fuel, depending on the feedstock and production pathway. While airlines have expressed willingness to incorporate SAF into their fuel mix, the price premium makes it economically unfeasible to do so at significant volumes without external support mechanisms. Government mandates, such as the European Union's ReFuelEU regulation requiring a minimum SAF blending ratio of 2 percent by 2025 and 6 percent by 2030, provide demand certainty but also raise concerns about potential cost pass-through to consumers.

The report recommends a coordinated approach involving production capacity investments, carbon pricing mechanisms that make conventional fuels reflect their true environmental cost, and public-private partnerships to de-risk early-stage SAF production facilities. Without such coordinated action, the report warns, the aviation industry will fall significantly short of its decarbonization commitments.
