# Crisis Management Protocol — Operational Continuity and Stakeholder Communications Framework

TO: All Division Heads, Regional Managing Directors, and Crisis Response Team Members
FROM: Office of the Chief Operating Officer, Kensington-Aldridge Holdings PLC
RE: Activation and Implementation of Revised Crisis Management Protocol (CMP-R4)

This memorandum supersedes all previously issued crisis management directives and establishes the definitive framework governing the organization's response to material operational disruptions, reputational threats, and regulatory enforcement events. The revised protocol reflects lessons learned from the supply chain disruption incident of Q3 2025, during which the absence of clearly delineated escalation pathways and communication protocols resulted in a 72-hour delay in stakeholder notification that subsequently attracted regulatory censure and an estimated $45 million in reputational damage.

Severity Classification and Escalation: All potential crisis events shall be classified within 60 minutes of initial identification according to a four-tier severity matrix. Tier 1 (Routine) events, defined as incidents with localized operational impact and no anticipated media or regulatory attention, shall be managed at the divisional level with notification to the regional managing director. Tier 2 (Elevated) events, involving multi-site operational disruption or potential regulatory inquiry, require immediate escalation to the Chief Operating Officer and activation of the Regional Crisis Response Team. Tier 3 (Severe) events, encompassing material financial impact, significant reputational exposure, or confirmed regulatory investigation, mandate activation of the Executive Crisis Committee and engagement of external crisis advisory consultants within four hours. Tier 4 (Critical) events — those posing existential threats to the organization's operational continuity, license to operate, or stakeholder trust — require immediate convocation of the full board of directors and retention of specialized legal counsel.

Stakeholder Communication Principles: All external communications during a crisis event must be coordinated exclusively through the Corporate Communications function in consultation with the General Counsel's office. No employee, regardless of seniority, is authorized to issue public statements, respond to media inquiries, or communicate with regulatory authorities without prior approval through the designated communication chain. Initial holding statements must be issued within four hours of Tier 3 or Tier 4 classification, with substantive updates provided at intervals not exceeding twelve hours until the crisis has been formally downgraded or resolved.

Post-Crisis Review: A comprehensive after-action review shall be conducted within thirty days of formal crisis resolution, with findings and recommendations presented to the board's Risk Committee.

## Q1. What event prompted the revision of the crisis management protocol?
- A. A cybersecurity breach that compromised customer financial data
- B. A supply chain disruption in which delayed stakeholder notification caused regulatory censure and reputational damage
- C. A hostile takeover attempt by a competing corporation
- D. A natural disaster that destroyed the company's headquarters
- Answer: B
- Explanation: The memo references "the supply chain disruption incident of Q3 2025, during which the absence of clearly delineated escalation pathways and communication protocols resulted in a 72-hour delay in stakeholder notification that subsequently attracted regulatory censure."

- D. Tier 4 — Critical events posing existential threats to the organization

- Explanation: Tier 4 (Critical) events "require immediate convocation of the full board of directors and retention of specialized legal counsel."

## Q4. The phrase "license to operate" in the Tier 4 description most likely refers to:
- A. A specific government permit required for manufacturing activities
- B. The organization's broad legitimacy and stakeholder acceptance enabling it to conduct business
- C. A software licensing agreement for operational technology systems
- D. The CEO's contractual authority to make executive decisions
- Answer: B
- Explanation: In a crisis management context, "license to operate" refers not to a literal license but to the organization's broader social, regulatory, and stakeholder legitimacy — the acceptance and trust that enables it to conduct its business activities.
