# Digital Asset Regulatory Framework

Regulatory Framework for Digital Asset Custody and Trading: Institutional Compliance Architecture

This memorandum provides a comprehensive analysis of the regulatory obligations applicable to institutional digital asset custody and trading operations, synthesizing requirements across the three primary jurisdictional frameworks that govern the firm's activities: the US Securities and Exchange Commission's regulatory guidance on digital asset securities, the European Union's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation ("MiCA"), and the Monetary Authority of Singapore's Payment Services Act as amended.

CLASSIFICATION AND REGISTRATION: The threshold regulatory challenge is the classification of digital assets, which determines the applicable regulatory regime. Under US law, the application of the Howey test to determine whether a digital asset constitutes an investment contract — and therefore a security — remains the dominant analytical framework, supplemented by the SEC's Framework for "Investment Contract" Analysis of Digital Assets. Under MiCA, digital assets are classified into three categories: asset-referenced tokens, e-money tokens, and utility tokens, each subject to distinct authorization requirements. The firm must maintain a dynamic classification register for all digital assets it custodies or trades, updated within 48 hours of any regulatory guidance, court decision, or legislative change that affects the classification of an asset.

CUSTODY REQUIREMENTS: Institutional custody of digital assets must satisfy four fundamental requirements that parallel but extend beyond traditional securities custody standards. First, segregation: client digital assets must be held in wallets that are cryptographically and operationally segregated from the firm's proprietary assets and from the assets of other clients, with on-chain verification of segregation available upon request. Second, key management: private keys controlling client assets must be generated and stored in hardware security modules certified to FIPS 140-2 Level 3 or equivalent, with no single individual having sole access to any key material sufficient to execute a transaction. Third, insurance: the firm must maintain digital asset custody insurance covering theft, hacking, and employee malfeasance, with coverage limits of no less than the greater of $100 million or 20 percent of the aggregate value of custodied assets. Fourth, disaster recovery: the firm must demonstrate the capability to restore access to all client assets within 72 hours of a catastrophic loss of primary infrastructure, validated through semi-annual testing.

TRANSACTION MONITORING AND TRAVEL RULE COMPLIANCE: All digital asset transactions must be screened against sanctions lists maintained by the US Office of Foreign Assets Control, the European Union, and the United Nations Security Council. The firm must implement the FATF Travel Rule requirements, transmitting originator and beneficiary information for all transfers exceeding $3,000 in value (under US rules) or EUR 1,000 (under EU rules). Blockchain analytics tools must be employed to assess the risk profile of counterparty wallet addresses, with transactions involving wallets flagged as high-risk subject to enhanced due diligence and, where necessary, filing of Suspicious Activity Reports.

## Q1. What determines which regulatory regime applies to a digital asset in the US?
- A. The asset's market capitalization
- B. The application of the Howey test to determine if it constitutes an investment contract
- C. The geographic location of the asset's issuer
- D. The number of holders of the asset
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Under US law, 'the application of the Howey test to determine whether a digital asset constitutes an investment contract' is the dominant framework.

- D. Multi-signature wallets managed exclusively by the CEO

- Explanation: Private keys must be stored in 'hardware security modules certified to FIPS 140-2 Level 3, with no single individual having sole access to any key material sufficient to execute a transaction.'
