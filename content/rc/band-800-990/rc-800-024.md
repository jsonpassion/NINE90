# Letter to Shareholders — Annual Report Fiscal Year 2025

Dear Fellow Shareholders,

Fiscal year 2025 was a pivotal year for Kensington-Aldridge Holdings, one in which we navigated significant macroeconomic headwinds while executing transformative strategic initiatives that we believe position the company for sustained long-term value creation. I am writing to provide you with a candid assessment of our performance, the rationale behind our key decisions, and our outlook for the year ahead.

Our consolidated revenue of $12.6 billion represented a 5 percent increase over the prior year, while adjusted earnings per share grew 9 percent to $7.42, reflecting the benefit of both operating leverage and disciplined capital allocation. However, I would be remiss not to acknowledge that our reported net income of $824 million included a $215 million non-cash impairment charge related to the goodwill associated with our 2022 acquisition of Vanguard Precision Technologies. The Vanguard business has underperformed the projections that supported the original acquisition valuation, primarily due to the delayed adoption of next-generation aerospace components by major airframe manufacturers.

We took decisive action to address the Vanguard situation. In September, we appointed a new division president, restructured the commercial team, and realigned the product development roadmap to focus on the defense and space sectors, where we see stronger near-term demand signals. While the impairment charge is a disappointment, we remain confident in the long-term strategic value of the Vanguard technology portfolio and its contribution to our integrated aerospace capabilities.

Our capital allocation framework continued to prioritize a balanced approach. We invested $1.1 billion in capital expenditures, including the completion of our advanced manufacturing center in Singapore, which is now operational and expected to generate $400 million in annual revenue at full capacity. We returned $680 million to shareholders through dividends and share repurchases, and we reduced net debt by $320 million, bringing our leverage ratio to 2.1 times EBITDA.

Looking forward, we see both opportunities and uncertainties. Order backlog stands at a record $28 billion, providing strong revenue visibility, but supply chain normalization remains uneven and geopolitical tensions continue to create unpredictable trade dynamics. We are cautiously optimistic about fiscal year 2026 and expect to deliver revenue growth of 4 to 7 percent and earnings per share growth of 8 to 12 percent.

Respectfully,
Catherine Harwell
Chair and Chief Executive Officer

## Q1. What caused the goodwill impairment charge?
- A. Aerospace customers delayed adoption of components that Vanguard was designed to produce.
- B. The Vanguard acquisition target committed accounting fraud.
- C. A natural disaster destroyed Vanguard's manufacturing facilities.
- D. Regulatory changes prohibited the sale of Vanguard's products.
- Answer: A
- Explanation: The impairment resulted from Vanguard underperforming "primarily due to the delayed adoption of next-generation aerospace components by major airframe manufacturers."

## Q2. How much was returned to shareholders during the fiscal year?
- A. $320 million
- B. $680 million
- C. $400 million
- D. $1.1 billion
- Answer: B
- Explanation: "We returned $680 million to shareholders through dividends and share repurchases."

## Q3. What can be inferred about the CEO's communication style in this letter?
- A. She avoids any mention of challenges or setbacks to maintain investor confidence.
- B. She provides a balanced and transparent assessment that acknowledges both successes and disappointments.
- C. She blames external factors exclusively for all performance shortfalls.
- D. She focuses entirely on future projections without addressing current results.
- Answer: B
- Explanation: The CEO states she provides "a candid assessment," acknowledges the impairment as "a disappointment," and discusses both achievements and headwinds.

## Q4. The phrase "revenue visibility" in the outlook section most likely refers to:
- A. The company's ability to see its competitors' revenue figures
- B. A requirement to publicly disclose revenue projections to regulators
- C. Confidence in predicting future revenue based on committed orders in the backlog
- D. The clarity of financial statements presented to auditors
- Answer: C
- Explanation: A "record $28 billion" backlog provides "strong revenue visibility," meaning the company can predict future revenue with greater confidence because orders are already committed.
