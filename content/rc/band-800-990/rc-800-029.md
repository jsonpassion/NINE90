# Institutional Investors Drive Corporate Governance Reforms

The relationship between institutional investors and corporate boards is undergoing a transformation that is reshaping governance practices across publicly traded companies. A new generation of active ownership strategies, extending well beyond traditional proxy voting, is compelling boards to adopt more rigorous oversight structures, enhance transparency, and align executive compensation more closely with long-term value creation.

The Institutional Shareholders Council, representing asset managers with combined assets under management of $38 trillion, released updated governance principles in August 2025 that establish significantly more demanding expectations for portfolio companies. The principles require that independent directors constitute at least 75 percent of the board, up from the widely accepted standard of simple majority independence. They also mandate that board chairs be independent of management, effectively ending the practice of combining the chair and CEO roles that remains common among large U.S. corporations.

Perhaps the most consequential reform involves executive compensation. The updated principles call for elimination of single-trigger change-of-control provisions that accelerate equity vesting upon a change in corporate ownership regardless of whether the executive's employment is terminated. Instead, the Council advocates for double-trigger provisions that require both a change of control and involuntary termination before vesting acceleration occurs. The principles also recommend that at least 50 percent of long-term incentive compensation be tied to performance metrics with a minimum three-year measurement period, with clawback provisions extending five years beyond the vesting date.

Empirical research supports the institutional investors' position. A study by the Stanford Corporate Governance Research Institute, analyzing 1,200 companies over a ten-year period, found that firms adopting the governance reforms advocated by institutional investors experienced 14 percent higher total shareholder returns than peer companies that maintained conventional governance structures. The researchers attributed the outperformance primarily to improved capital allocation decisions and reduced agency costs resulting from more effective board oversight.

Critics contend that overly prescriptive governance mandates may not be appropriate for all companies and could disadvantage firms in specific industries or stages of development where concentrated authority enables faster decision-making. They advocate for a principles-based approach that allows boards flexibility to deviate from specific governance standards when supported by a compelling rationale.

## Q1. What is a "double-trigger" provision in the context of executive compensation?
- A. A provision requiring two separate shareholder votes to approve compensation packages
- B. A requirement that both a change of corporate control and involuntary termination occur before equity vesting accelerates
- C. A mechanism that doubles an executive's base salary upon promotion
- D. A tax provision that triggers penalties on excessive compensation payments
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Double-trigger provisions "require both a change of control and involuntary termination before vesting acceleration occurs."

## Q2. What percentage of total shareholder return outperformance was attributed to governance reforms?
- A. 10 percent
- B. 38 percent
- C. 14 percent
- D. 75 percent
- Answer: C
- Explanation: Firms adopting the reforms "experienced 14 percent higher total shareholder returns than peer companies."

## Q3. What can be inferred about the current state of CEO-chair role separation in U.S. companies?
- A. All U.S. companies have already separated the roles.
- B. U.S. law prohibits combining the roles but enforcement is weak.
- C. Combining the chair and CEO roles remains common and the new principles seek to end this practice.
- D. Only technology companies still combine the roles.
- Answer: C
- Explanation: The principles mandate independent board chairs, "effectively ending the practice of combining the chair and CEO roles that remains common among large U.S. corporations."

## Q4. The term "agency costs" in the research findings most likely refers to:
- A. Costs arising from conflicts of interest between corporate managers and shareholders
- B. Fees paid to external recruitment agencies for hiring executives
- C. Travel and entertainment expenses incurred by board members
- D. Legal fees for government regulatory compliance
- Answer: A
- Explanation: In corporate governance theory, "agency costs" are costs arising from the misalignment of interests between management (agents) and shareholders (principals), which better oversight aims to reduce.
