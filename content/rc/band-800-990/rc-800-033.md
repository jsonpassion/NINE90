# Regulatory Analysis: Proposed Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism — Impact on Trade-Exposed Industries

The proposed Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism ("CBAM"), scheduled for full implementation in January 2027, represents one of the most significant developments in international trade policy in decades. By imposing a carbon cost on imported goods equivalent to the domestic carbon price faced by domestic producers, the mechanism seeks to prevent "carbon leakage" — the relocation of production to jurisdictions with less stringent climate regulations — while maintaining the competitiveness of domestic manufacturers.

Under the proposed framework, importers of designated goods, initially covering steel, aluminum, cement, fertilizers, electricity, and hydrogen, must purchase CBAM certificates corresponding to the embedded carbon emissions of their imports. The certificate price will be linked to the domestic emissions trading system price, currently averaging €85 per metric ton of CO2 equivalent. Importers may claim a reduction in their CBAM obligation to the extent that the goods have already been subject to a carbon price in the country of origin.

The economic impact analysis prepared by the Trade Policy Research Institute projects that the CBAM will increase import costs for affected goods by an average of 8 to 14 percent, with the most significant impact on imports from countries that lack domestic carbon pricing mechanisms. Steel imports from countries without carbon pricing would face the full CBAM charge, estimated at approximately €170 per ton of finished steel, representing a 12 percent cost increase at current market prices.

Trade-exposed industries in exporting countries are responding with a range of strategies. Several major steel-producing nations have announced plans to accelerate the implementation of domestic carbon pricing systems, which would reduce their exporters' CBAM obligations. Others have signaled their intention to challenge the mechanism at the World Trade Organization, arguing that it constitutes a discriminatory trade barrier inconsistent with WTO non-discrimination principles.

Proponents argue that the CBAM is essential to maintain the environmental integrity of ambitious climate policies and that its design, which credits carbon prices already paid in the country of origin, is consistent with WTO obligations. The ultimate resolution of these competing claims is likely to shape the landscape of international climate and trade policy for decades.

## Q1. What is the primary objective of the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism?
- A. To generate revenue for climate adaptation programs
- B. To impose trade sanctions on countries with high carbon emissions
- C. To prevent carbon leakage by equalizing carbon costs between domestic and imported goods
- D. To subsidize domestic manufacturers' transition to clean energy
- Answer: C
- Explanation: The CBAM "imposing a carbon cost on imported goods equivalent to the domestic carbon price" seeks to prevent "carbon leakage — the relocation of production to jurisdictions with less stringent climate regulations."

## Q2. How can importers reduce their CBAM obligation?
- A. By purchasing carbon offset credits from reforestation projects
- B. By relocating their production facilities to the importing country
- C. By committing to reduce emissions within five years
- D. By demonstrating that their goods were already subject to a carbon price in the country of origin
- Answer: D
- Explanation: "Importers may claim a reduction in their CBAM obligation to the extent that the goods have already been subject to a carbon price in the country of origin."

## Q3. What can be inferred about the geopolitical consequences of the CBAM?
- A. The mechanism is creating incentives for some countries to adopt carbon pricing while others prepare legal challenges.
- B. All exporting countries have expressed unanimous support for the mechanism.
- C. The CBAM will have no effect on international trade relationships.
- D. Exporting countries have agreed to implement identical carbon prices simultaneously.
- Answer: A
- Explanation: Some countries "announced plans to accelerate implementation of domestic carbon pricing systems" while others "signaled their intention to challenge the mechanism at the World Trade Organization."

## Q4. The phrase "carbon leakage" in this context refers to:
- A. Physical emissions escaping from carbon capture equipment
- B. The shift of production to countries with weaker climate regulations, undermining emission reduction efforts
- C. Information about carbon prices being leaked to unauthorized parties
- D. The gradual decline in carbon pricing revenue over time
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Carbon leakage is explicitly defined as "the relocation of production to jurisdictions with less stringent climate regulations."
