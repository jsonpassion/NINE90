# Confidential Memorandum — Whistleblower Protection and Internal Investigation Protocols

TO: Board of Directors, Audit Committee
FROM: General Counsel
DATE: November 25, 2025
RE: Enhancements to Whistleblower Protection Framework and Internal Investigation Procedures

This memorandum recommends comprehensive enhancements to the company's whistleblower protection and internal investigation frameworks, prompted by recent regulatory guidance from the SEC's Office of the Whistleblower and the Department of Justice's updated Corporate Enforcement Policy. Both agencies have signaled intensified scrutiny of corporate programs that may, intentionally or through structural inadequacy, discourage employees from reporting potential violations or cooperating with government investigations.

The SEC's September 2025 Enforcement Advisory specifically identified three corporate practices that the Commission views as potentially constituting impermissible impediments to whistleblowing: (1) separation agreements that require departing employees to waive the right to receive whistleblower awards; (2) internal reporting policies that mandate pre-filing disclosure to the company before an employee may contact a regulatory agency; and (3) confidentiality provisions in employment agreements that are drafted so broadly as to potentially encompass communications with government regulators about potential securities violations.

Our external counsel's review of our current agreements and policies has identified areas of concern in each of these categories. Specifically, our standard executive separation agreement contains a provision that could be interpreted as conditioning severance payments on the executive's waiver of rights to government whistleblower awards, although this was not the drafting intent. Additionally, our internal reporting policy states that employees "should" report concerns internally before contacting external authorities, language that the SEC has indicated may be perceived as a mandatory pre-filing requirement, notwithstanding the use of precatory rather than mandatory language.

We recommend the following corrective actions: revision of all separation agreements to explicitly preserve whistleblower rights; amendment of the internal reporting policy to clarify that internal reporting is encouraged but never required as a precondition for contacting regulatory authorities; review and revision of confidentiality provisions in all employment agreements; and mandatory training for all managers on anti-retaliation obligations. The estimated cost of implementing these enhancements is $340,000, a modest investment relative to the potential enforcement consequences and reputational damage that could result from regulatory action in this area.

- Explanation: The review was "prompted by recent regulatory guidance from the SEC's Office of the Whistleblower and the Department of Justice's updated Corporate Enforcement Policy."

## Q2. What specific language issue was identified in the internal reporting policy?
- A. The policy explicitly prohibits employees from contacting any external authorities.
- B. The policy requires employees to obtain board approval before making any report.

- Answer: D
- Explanation: The policy states employees "should" report internally first, "language that the SEC has indicated may be perceived as a mandatory pre-filing requirement, notwithstanding the use of precatory rather than mandatory language."

## Q3. What can be inferred about the distinction between "precatory" and "mandatory" language?
- A. There is no meaningful legal distinction between the two.
- B. Only "mandatory" language can be included in company policies.
- C. "Precatory" language is always legally binding in employment agreements.
- D. "Precatory" language recommends without requiring, but regulators may still view it as coercive in practice.
- Answer: D
- Explanation: The memo uses "precatory" to describe the "should" language (recommending, not requiring), but notes the SEC may still perceive it as a mandatory requirement, showing the gap between intent and regulatory interpretation.
