# Regulatory Stress Test Results — Consolidated Banking Group Performance

The Federal Reserve Board has published the results of its 2025 supervisory stress test for the nation's 30 largest banking organizations, assessing their ability to maintain adequate capital levels under a severely adverse macroeconomic scenario. This analysis summarizes the results for Consolidated Financial Holdings ("CFH"), a globally systemic important banking institution with total assets of $1.8 trillion.

Under the severely adverse scenario, which models a deep recession with unemployment rising to 10.8 percent, a 40 percent decline in equity markets, and a 25 percent decrease in residential real estate prices, CFH's Common Equity Tier 1 ("CET1") capital ratio would decline from its current level of 12.4 percent to a minimum of 7.8 percent. This minimum level exceeds the regulatory requirement of 4.5 percent plus CFH's institution-specific stress capital buffer of 2.5 percent, for a combined minimum of 7.0 percent. Accordingly, CFH passes the stress test and maintains a buffer of 80 basis points above the requirement at the point of maximum stress.

The principal driver of capital depletion under the stress scenario is projected credit losses of $48 billion, concentrated in the commercial real estate ($18.2 billion), credit card ($12.4 billion), and commercial and industrial lending ($9.8 billion) portfolios. Net revenue under stress is projected at $22 billion, partially offsetting losses but insufficient to prevent substantial capital erosion.

CFH's performance represents an improvement over the 2024 stress test, in which the minimum CET1 ratio was projected at 7.2 percent. The improvement reflects the bank's deliberate reduction in commercial real estate exposure during the past year, with total CRE loans declining by $14 billion to $82 billion, and the strengthening of loss reserves for the credit card portfolio.

The stress test results will inform the Federal Reserve's determination of CFH's maximum permissible capital distribution, including dividends and share repurchases, for the four-quarter period beginning October 1, 2025. Based on the results, CFH is expected to receive authorization to maintain its current quarterly dividend of $1.20 per share and to resume share repurchases of up to $4 billion over the four-quarter period.

## Q1. What is CFH's stress capital buffer above the minimum requirement?
- A. 0.40 percentage points
- B. 2.50 percentage points
- C. 0.80 percentage points
- D. 4.50 percentage points
- Answer: C
- Explanation: The minimum CET1 under stress is 7.8 percent, and the combined requirement is 7.0 percent, leaving "a buffer of 80 basis points above the requirement."

## Q2. What was the largest category of projected credit losses?
- A. Credit card loans at $12.4 billion
- B. Commercial and industrial lending at $9.8 billion
- C. Commercial real estate at $18.2 billion
- D. Residential mortgage loans
- Answer: C
- Explanation: Commercial real estate losses were projected at $18.2 billion, the largest of the identified categories.

## Q3. What can be inferred about CFH's risk management strategy over the past year?
- A. The bank increased its exposure to commercial real estate to boost returns.
- B. The bank made no changes to its portfolio composition.
- C. The bank proactively reduced risk in its most vulnerable portfolios, improving stress test results.
- D. The bank failed the stress test and was required to raise additional capital.
- Answer: C
- Explanation: CFH deliberately reduced CRE exposure by $14 billion and strengthened credit card reserves, improving the minimum CET1 ratio from 7.2 to 7.8 percent.

## Q4. The phrase "capital distribution" in the final paragraph refers to:
- A. The return of capital to shareholders through dividends and share repurchases
- B. The dispersal of financial resources to branch locations for operational needs
- C. The allocation of government capital assistance to smaller banks
- D. The distribution of regulatory capital requirements across business divisions
- Answer: A
- Explanation: Capital distribution is explicitly defined as "including dividends and share repurchases" — returning capital to shareholders.
