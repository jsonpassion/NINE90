# Central Bank Digital Currencies: Implications for Commercial Banking

The prospect of central bank digital currencies being introduced in major economies has prompted an intensive examination of their potential impact on the commercial banking sector. A research paper published by the Institute for Monetary Policy Studies analyzes the various design choices available to central banks and assesses their likely consequences for bank funding models, payment systems, and financial stability.

The paper distinguishes between two fundamental CBDC design approaches: a direct model, in which the central bank manages all customer accounts and transactions, and an intermediated model, in which commercial banks distribute and manage CBDC wallets on behalf of the central bank. The direct model would represent the more disruptive option, potentially disintermediating commercial banks from the payment process entirely and threatening their access to low-cost deposit funding, which currently forms the foundation of their lending operations.

The intermediated model, which most central banks studying CBDCs appear to favor, would preserve the commercial banking sector's role in customer-facing services while providing the central bank with enhanced capabilities for monetary policy implementation and financial surveillance. Under this model, commercial banks would act as regulated intermediaries, managing CBDC wallets, processing transactions, and applying know-your-customer and anti-money-laundering procedures. The paper estimates that this approach would reduce commercial bank deposit bases by 5 to 15 percent, as some consumers and businesses migrate a portion of their holdings to CBDC wallets, attracted by the perceived safety of a central bank liability.

The paper recommends that commercial banks begin preparing for the introduction of CBDCs by developing CBDC-compatible payment infrastructure, diversifying their funding sources to reduce dependence on retail deposits, and exploring new value-added services that can be built on top of CBDC platforms. The authors note that while CBDCs present challenges, they also create opportunities for banks that position themselves as innovative intermediaries in the evolving digital monetary landscape.

## Q1. What is the key difference between the direct and intermediated CBDC models?
- A. The direct model uses blockchain while the intermediated model does not
- B. The direct model applies only to retail customers while the intermediated model includes businesses
- C. The direct model is limited to domestic transactions while the intermediated model supports international transfers
- D. In the direct model, the central bank manages all accounts; in the intermediated model, commercial banks handle distribution
- Answer: D
- Explanation: The paper distinguishes between "a direct model, in which the central bank manages all customer accounts" and "an intermediated model, in which commercial banks distribute and manage CBDC wallets."

## Q2. By how much could the intermediated model reduce commercial bank deposit bases?
- A. 1 to 3 percent
- B. 5 to 15 percent
- C. 15 to 25 percent
- D. 25 to 40 percent
- Answer: B
- Explanation: The paper estimates "this approach would reduce commercial bank deposit bases by 5 to 15 percent."

## Q3. Which CBDC model do most central banks appear to favor?
- A. The direct model
- B. The intermediated model
- C. A decentralized model
- D. No specific preference has emerged
- Answer: B
- Explanation: The paper states "The intermediated model, which most central banks studying CBDCs appear to favor."

## Q4. What opportunity does the paper identify for commercial banks?
- A. Becoming central bank subsidiaries
- B. Eliminating their digital banking services
- C. Positioning as innovative intermediaries in the digital monetary landscape
- D. Merging with fintech competitors
- Answer: C
- Explanation: The paper notes "opportunities for banks that position themselves as innovative intermediaries in the evolving digital monetary landscape."
