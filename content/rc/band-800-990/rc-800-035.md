# Confidential Memorandum — Enhanced Sanctions Compliance Program

TO: Board of Directors and Executive Committee
FROM: General Counsel and Chief Compliance Officer
DATE: November 18, 2025
RE: Mandatory Enhancements to the Corporate Sanctions Compliance Program

This memorandum summarizes the findings of a comprehensive review of our sanctions compliance program conducted by external counsel in response to the U.S. Department of the Treasury's Office of Foreign Assets Control ("OFAC") enforcement trends and the recent expansion of sanctions programs targeting multiple jurisdictions. The review identified several areas where our existing compliance infrastructure must be strengthened to mitigate the risk of inadvertent sanctions violations that could expose the corporation to civil penalties, criminal prosecution, and severe reputational damage.

The most critical finding concerns the adequacy of our counterparty screening processes. Our current sanctions screening system, implemented in 2021, cross-references transaction counterparties against the OFAC Specially Designated Nationals list and consolidated EU sanctions lists. However, the system does not screen against the full spectrum of sanctions-relevant databases, including the sectoral sanctions identifications list, the non-SDN menu-based sanctions list, and the lists maintained by the United Nations Security Council and the UK Office of Financial Sanctions Implementation. The review further revealed that 14 percent of transactions processed through our Asian operations during the past 12 months bypassed the screening system entirely due to a configuration error in the regional payment processing platform.

The second finding relates to beneficial ownership verification. Current procedures verify the direct ownership of counterparties but do not adequately trace indirect ownership chains to identify entities that are 50 percent or more owned, individually or in aggregate, by sanctioned persons. OFAC's 50 Percent Rule requires that entities owned 50 percent or more by blocked persons are themselves treated as blocked, even if not specifically listed on any sanctions list. Our compliance team identified three instances during the review period where transactions were processed with entities that, upon deeper ownership analysis, were indirectly controlled by sanctioned individuals.

Recommended enhancements include: upgrading the screening system to incorporate all relevant sanctions databases ($2.8 million); implementing automated beneficial ownership tracing technology ($1.6 million); conducting retroactive screening of all transactions processed through Asian operations during the affected period; and establishing a dedicated sanctions compliance unit staffed by five full-time specialists reporting to the Chief Compliance Officer.

## Q1. What was the most critical compliance gap identified?
- A. Inadequate counterparty screening that missed multiple sanctions databases and bypassed 14 percent of Asian transactions
- B. The absence of any sanctions screening system
- C. A single employee who intentionally circumvented sanctions controls
- D. The company's decision to cease operations in sanctioned jurisdictions
- Answer: A
- Explanation: The screening system did not cover the full spectrum of databases, and "14 percent of transactions processed through our Asian operations bypassed the screening system entirely."

## Q2. What does OFAC's 50 Percent Rule require?
- A. Entities owned 50 percent or more by sanctioned persons must be treated as blocked, even if not specifically listed
- B. Companies must limit business with any foreign entity to 50 percent of total revenue
- C. Sanctions compliance budgets must equal at least 50 percent of legal department spending
- D. Companies must screen at least 50 percent of their transactions for sanctions violations
- Answer: A
- Explanation: "OFAC's 50 Percent Rule requires that entities owned 50 percent or more by blocked persons are themselves treated as blocked, even if not specifically listed on any sanctions list."

## Q3. What can be inferred about the potential consequences of the identified gaps?
- A. The company has already been penalized by OFAC for these specific violations.
- B. The compliance gaps had no practical impact on the company's operations.
- C. The gaps could have facilitated inadvertent transactions with sanctioned parties, creating significant legal and financial risk.
- D. Only the Asian operations are subject to sanctions regulations.
- Answer: C
- Explanation: Three transactions were processed with indirectly sanctioned entities, and 14 percent of Asian transactions bypassed screening — creating real exposure to "civil penalties, criminal prosecution, and severe reputational damage."

## Q4. The phrase "inadvertent sanctions violations" suggests:
- A. The company intentionally violated sanctions for financial gain
- B. Violations occurred unintentionally due to inadequate compliance systems rather than deliberate wrongdoing
- C. The violations were too minor to warrant any regulatory response
- D. The company was unaware that sanctions programs existed
- Answer: B
- Explanation: "Inadvertent" means unintentional, and the memo attributes the gaps to system limitations and configuration errors rather than deliberate circumvention.
