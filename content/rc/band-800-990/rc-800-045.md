# Private Credit Market Expansion Raises Systemic Risk Questions

The rapid expansion of the private credit market, which has grown from $800 billion in 2020 to an estimated $2.1 trillion in 2025, is prompting regulators and market participants to assess whether the sector's growth trajectory introduces systemic risks to the broader financial system. Private credit funds, which provide financing directly to borrowers outside the traditional banking system, have captured a significant share of the corporate lending market previously dominated by banks and syndicated loan investors.

The sector's growth has been facilitated by several structural factors. Post-2008 banking regulations, including higher capital requirements and enhanced regulatory oversight, have reduced banks' appetite for leveraged lending, creating a supply gap that private credit funds have filled. Simultaneously, institutional investors seeking higher yields in a persistently low-interest-rate environment channeled unprecedented capital into private credit strategies, providing funds with ample dry powder to deploy.

The Financial Stability Board's November 2025 report identifies three primary risk vectors. First, the opacity of private credit portfolios, which are not subject to the same disclosure requirements as publicly traded debt or bank loan portfolios, limits regulators' ability to monitor the aggregate buildup of credit risk. Second, the prevalence of floating-rate structures in private credit — estimated at 92 percent of outstanding loans — means that the sharp increase in interest rates since 2022 has significantly elevated borrower debt service burdens, with the proportion of borrowers whose interest coverage ratios have fallen below 1.0 times increasing from 5 percent to 18 percent over the past three years.

Third, the interconnectedness between private credit funds and the banking system through subscription lines, back-leverage facilities, and co-investment arrangements creates potential contagion channels. The FSB estimates that banks' direct and indirect exposure to the private credit sector totals approximately $450 billion, a figure that could amplify stress transmission during a credit cycle downturn.

Industry representatives argue that private credit's structural characteristics — including long-term fund structures that prevent investor redemption runs, active portfolio management with direct borrower relationships, and covenant packages that provide earlier warning signals than syndicated loans — make it inherently more resilient than its banking sector counterpart to liquidity-driven stress events.

## Q1. What has driven the rapid growth of private credit?
- A. Deregulation of the banking sector that encouraged banks to increase lending
- B. A decline in corporate borrowing demand that favored smaller lenders
- C. Government subsidies specifically designed to promote private credit funds
- D. Post-2008 banking regulations reducing bank lending appetite, combined with institutional investors seeking higher yields
- Answer: D
- Explanation: Banking regulations "reduced banks' appetite for leveraged lending" while "institutional investors seeking higher yields channeled unprecedented capital into private credit strategies."

## Q2. What percentage of private credit loans are floating-rate?
- A. 18 percent
- B. 92 percent
- C. 72 percent
- D. 45 percent
- Answer: B
- Explanation: "The prevalence of floating-rate structures in private credit — estimated at 92 percent of outstanding loans."

## Q3. What can be inferred about the potential for financial contagion from private credit?
- A. Private credit operates in complete isolation from the banking system.
- B. The $450 billion in bank exposure to private credit could transmit stress to the broader financial system during a downturn.
- C. Banks have no financial relationship with private credit funds.
- D. Contagion risk has been eliminated through regulatory measures.
- Answer: B
- Explanation: Banks' "$450 billion direct and indirect exposure" through various financial arrangements "could amplify stress transmission during a credit cycle downturn."

## Q4. The phrase "dry powder" in the context of private credit funds refers to:
- A. Chemical materials stored in warehouse facilities
- B. Fund management fees collected but not yet distributed
- C. Outstanding loans that have not yet been repaid
- D. Uncommitted capital available for deployment into new investments
- Answer: D
- Explanation: "Dry powder" is an investment term for capital that has been committed by investors but not yet deployed, giving funds resources to invest.
