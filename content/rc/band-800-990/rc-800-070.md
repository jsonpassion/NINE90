# Global Supply Chain Risk Assessment

Enterprise Supply Chain Geopolitical Risk Assessment — Strategic Vulnerability Analysis

Prepared by the Global Risk Intelligence Unit for the Chief Supply Chain Officer

This assessment evaluates the geopolitical risk exposure embedded in the organization's global supply chain, analyzing vulnerabilities across 47 critical supply pathways and 312 tier-one and tier-two suppliers operating in 28 countries. The analysis employs a proprietary risk-scoring methodology that integrates political stability indices, trade policy trajectory analysis, sanctions exposure probability, and logistical chokepoint concentration to generate a composite risk score for each supply pathway.

The assessment identifies three categories of elevated concern. Category A — Immediate Risk (12 to 24 months): The organization's dependence on Taiwanese semiconductor fabrication facilities for 68 percent of advanced microprocessor procurement represents the highest-severity vulnerability. The probability-weighted annual expected loss from a major disruption to this supply pathway is estimated at $340 million, reflecting both the concentration of sourcing and the 18-to-24-month lead time required to qualify alternative fabrication sources. Mitigation measures currently in progress include the qualification of a secondary fabrication facility in Arizona scheduled for initial production in Q4 2026, and the pre-positioning of 90 days of strategic inventory at an estimated carrying cost of $28 million annually.

Category B — Evolving Risk (24 to 48 months): Shifting trade policy dynamics between major economic blocs create medium-term uncertainty for approximately $1.2 billion in annual cross-border component flows. The proliferation of local content requirements, technology transfer restrictions, and tariff escalation scenarios could increase total landed costs by 8 to 15 percent for affected product lines. The risk intelligence unit has developed three scenario models — baseline, moderate disruption, and severe fragmentation — with probability weightings of 50, 35, and 15 percent respectively. Under the severe fragmentation scenario, the organization would need to establish manufacturing redundancy in at least two additional regions at an estimated capital cost of $180 million.

Category C — Structural Risk (beyond 48 months): Long-term decarbonization policies across major markets will progressively alter the economic viability of existing supply chain configurations. Carbon border adjustment mechanisms, already enacted in the European Union and under legislative consideration in three additional jurisdictions, will impose effective tariffs on the embedded carbon content of imported goods. Preliminary modeling indicates that the organization's current supply chain carbon intensity would result in border adjustment charges of approximately $45 million annually under the EU mechanism alone, increasing to an estimated $120 million if comparable mechanisms are adopted in North America and Japan.
