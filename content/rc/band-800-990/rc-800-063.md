# Corporate Governance Reform Proposal

Proposal for Board of Directors Governance Reform — Enhancing Oversight Effectiveness and Stakeholder Accountability

Submitted by the Nominating and Corporate Governance Committee to the Board of Directors, Pinnacle Holdings International PLC

This proposal sets forth a comprehensive reform of the governance structures and practices of the Board of Directors, developed in response to the findings of the independent governance review commissioned in September 2025. The review, conducted by Ashworth Governance Advisors, identified several areas where the Board's practices fall below emerging best-practice standards, potentially exposing the company to regulatory risk and undermining investor confidence during a period of heightened scrutiny of corporate governance quality.

The first area of reform concerns board composition and independence. Currently, 7 of the Board's 12 members qualify as independent under applicable listing standards. However, three of these nominally independent directors have served for more than 15 years, and governance researchers have documented that extended tenure can gradually erode the objectivity and constructive skepticism that independent directors are expected to bring. We propose implementing a maximum tenure of 12 years for independent directors, with a transitional provision allowing the three affected directors to serve through the conclusion of their current terms. Additionally, we recommend expanding the Board to 14 members by adding two directors with specific expertise in cybersecurity and environmental sustainability, reflecting the growing materiality of these topics to the company's risk profile and strategic direction.

The second reform addresses executive compensation oversight. The current practice of engaging a single compensation consultant appointed by management creates an inherent conflict of interest. We propose that the Compensation Committee engage its own independent advisor, selected through a competitive process and reporting exclusively to the Committee. Furthermore, we recommend that at least 40 percent of senior executive variable compensation be linked to non-financial performance metrics, including employee engagement, carbon emissions reduction, and customer satisfaction indices, thereby aligning incentive structures with the company's stated commitment to long-term stakeholder value creation.

The third area concerns risk oversight. We propose the establishment of a dedicated Risk Committee, separate from the Audit Committee, with a mandate to oversee enterprise risk management, including emerging risks related to artificial intelligence deployment, supply chain resilience, and geopolitical instability. The Risk Committee would meet at least six times annually and would have authority to commission independent assessments of any risk area it deems material.

Implementation of these reforms is projected to increase annual governance-related costs by approximately $1.4 million, primarily attributable to additional director compensation, independent advisory fees, and enhanced reporting infrastructure. We consider this investment modest relative to the potential costs of governance failures, which industry data suggests can result in market capitalization losses averaging 7.5 percent following significant governance-related controversies.

## Q1. Why is extended director tenure considered problematic?
- A. It violates securities regulations in most jurisdictions.
- B. Governance researchers have documented that it can erode objectivity and constructive skepticism.
- C. Long-serving directors demand excessively high compensation.
- D. It prevents the board from meeting quorum requirements.
- Answer: B
- Explanation: The proposal notes 'governance researchers have documented that extended tenure can gradually erode the objectivity and constructive skepticism that independent directors are expected to bring.'

## Q2. What conflict of interest does the proposal identify in executive compensation oversight?
- A. Board members setting their own salaries
- B. The CEO serving as chairman of the Compensation Committee
- C. A single compensation consultant appointed by management advising the Committee
- D. Executive bonuses being paid before shareholder approval
- Answer: C
- Explanation: The proposal states 'engaging a single compensation consultant appointed by management creates an inherent conflict of interest.'

## Q3. What is the proposed composition of senior executive variable compensation?
- A. 100 percent based on financial performance metrics
- B. At least 40 percent linked to non-financial metrics such as employee engagement and carbon reduction
- C. 50 percent in stock options and 50 percent in cash bonuses
- D. Entirely discretionary based on board assessment
- Answer: B
- Explanation: The proposal recommends 'at least 40 percent of senior executive variable compensation be linked to non-financial performance metrics.'

## Q4. How does the proposal justify the $1.4 million annual cost increase?
- A. By comparing it to competitors' governance spending
- B. By noting that governance failures can cause average market capitalization losses of 7.5 percent
- C. By demonstrating immediate revenue gains from improved governance
- D. By citing regulatory requirements mandating these reforms
- Answer: B
- Explanation: The cost is 'modest relative to the potential costs of governance failures, which industry data suggests can result in market capitalization losses averaging 7.5 percent.'
